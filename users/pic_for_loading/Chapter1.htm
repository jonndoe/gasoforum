The reason for having them grouped in such a way is that the gases have different characteristics. The most important factors in this situation are the boiling point, reactivity, toxicity, inflammability and chemical properties. We shall now look a little more closely at the individual gas groups.

LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas)

LPG gases are produced by the fractionating of natural gases, and are also extracted from crude oil as a byproduct of the distillation processes at oil refineries. Between 1% and 3% of the volume of crude oil consists of LPG. The term "LPG" is used in the oil industries' terminology for a number of hydrocarbons or mixtures of these, mainly propane and butane. This is a badly chosen expression, since each type of hydrocarbon (petroleum gas) can be in liquid form under the right conditions.

The American Petroleum Institute (API) defines LPG as one of the following hydrocarbons, or eventually a mixture of these gases:

Propane
Propylene
Normal butane
Iso-butane
Butylene

All of these gases have a boiling point of between 0_C and _50C at atmospheric conditions. The boiling point of LPG is essentially higher than that of LEG and LNG. LPG has also a higher density and, therefore, demands a stronger cargo tank..

LPG is commonly used as a raw material in the petro chemical industry and also for energy purposes such as fuel, heating, lighting etc.

NGL (Natural Gas Liquids)

NGL, or wet gas, is the name used to denote the gases which are found dissolved in crude oil. During the separation of crude oil a mixture of gases is released, consisting of ethane, propane and butane, together with heavier hydrocarbons. The composition of this gas varies from one oil field to another. This wet gas is used, amongst other things, in the production of ethylene in the petrochemical industry.

LEG (Liquefied Ethylene Gas)

LEG is the abbreviated symbol used to denote condensed ethylene gas. Ethylene is not found naturally, but is a semi-manufactured product produced from oil, wet gas, or from the gases ethane or propane. The boiling point of ethylene is _1040C. Ethylene is a raw material which is used in the production of a great many products. For example: plastics, food colourings, polyethylene foam, styrene, and also for welding and cutting of metals..

LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas)

LNG, or natural gas, is a mixture of gases which is found in the earth in the same way as oil. LNG consists mainly of methane which is the lightest of the hydrocarbons. Also, LNG contains smaller quantities of, amongst other things, ethane, propane and butane. At atmospheric pressure, natural gas has a boiling point of approx. _1600C, and is mainly used for energy purposes as a substitute for coal and oil. A certain amount of natural gas is also used in the production of ammonia and fertilizers for agricultural purposes.

Ammonia (NH3)

Ammonia has a boiling point of _330C at atmospheric pressure, and is the combustion product of oil and gas. Ammonia can also be produced from natural gases with the use of a catalysing process. The principal use of ammonia is as a raw material in the fertilizer industry, and also in the productions of plastics, explosives, dyes and cleaning fluids.

Chlorine (CL2)

Chlorine has a boiling point of _340C at atmospheric pressure. Chlorine cannot be found naturally, but can be produced, for example, by electrolysis of a sodium chloride solution (salt). Chlorine gas is used in the production of various chemicals and as a bleaching agent in the paper and cellulose industries.

In addition to the gases which are mentioned here, we have a number of products from the chemical industry which fall under the definition of gas, and can be transported by gas ships. These gases do not belong to any of the afore-mentioned group headings, and can be gases such as vinyl chloride (VCM), propadiene and butadiene, for instance.

The transport temperatures of some of the above-mentioned gases will vary, dependent on their purity and different mixtures. The required transport temperature will determine the pressure.

The ships from groups 1 and 2 are usually described as LPG-ships. Constructionwise these ships are very much alike. Simply there is talk of a difference in the choice of materials, insulation and cooling systems. As a consequence LPG and LEG ships are treated as a group.

Ships belonging to group 3 can usually transport only LNG, but smaller and medium-large LNG-ships have also been built for the transporting of LEG and LPG to increase their cargo flexibility.. Chlorine ships are essentially separated from the above for several reasons, among others that the chlorine gas is not explosive, but on the other hand chlorine is a dangerous health risk, even in very small concentrations..

Fully Pressurised Gas Ships (LPG)

The first gas ship that was built was fully pressurised, and such ships dominated the market up until the beginning of the 1960's. Today few of this type of ship are built, but they will still be in demand for years to come.

Fully pressurised ships (see fig. 1) are built up to the size of approx. 2000m3. As the cargo is transported at ambient temperature, these ships have no cooling system for the cargo, and the cargo tanks are not insulated. The ships can transport LPG and ammonia along with a number of other gases, but not LEG and LNG, because these gases cannot be liquefied at ambient temperature.

The tanks are normally designed for an internal pressure of 17,5 bar. For pure propane this pressure corresponds with its boiling point of 500C. The tanks are designed as vertical or horizontal cylinders arranged both above and below deck, and are stored using two or more cradle-like supports. There are also in existence fully pressurised ships with spherical tanks.

The tanks below deck are equipped with a "dome" which protrudes above deck.. All the necessary connections for loading and discharging, testing, liquid level devices, the temperature and the pressure are channeled through this dome.

The pipe system on this type of ship is normally quite simple, and consists of a combined loading and discharging line, and a vapour return line.

Fully pressurised ships do not have discharge pumps in the tanks. The discharge is achieved by building up an excess of pressure inside the tanks, and then "squeezing" the contents out of the tank. The excess pressure is achieved, if necessary, with a compressor on board or from shore.

On deck there are often arranged so-called booster-pumps to increase the pressure on the liquid further, which may be necessary in situations where the land-storage tanks lie a large distance away from the discharge site, or if they stand considerably higher than the ship.

Fully pressurised ships have both advantages and disadvantages:

Advantages:

One can use normal types of steel in the construction of the tanks due to the "normal" cargo temperatures.

Insulation of the tanks and loading lines is unnecessary when the cargo is being transported at ambient temperature.

There is no need for a refrigeration system on board.

There is little or no need for any auxiliary equipment since the pressure in the tanks is so high, and the cargo can be discharged using the vapour pressure instead of discharging pumps.

The ships are easy to operate, since the cargo under normal conditions does not need attention during the journey.

Secondary barriers (double tanks) are not required since the tanks are built so strongly that large leakages will not occur suddenly.

Disadvantages:

Limited use of the hull since the tanks are usually in the shape of cylinders or spheres.

Economic cargo capacity is limited to approx. 2000m3.

Heavy tank construction due to high tank pressure, and therefore large displacements in relation to the ships "dead weight".

The ship's trade is usually limited to coastal services.

Without tanks on the decks the ship makes relatively poor use of the hull.

The number of tons of cargo that can be transported at the same filling level and size of ship is smaller than that of a semipressurised or an atmospheric pressure ship. This can be illustrated by looking, for example, at propane, which has a 16% higher density at _420C than at +200C.

Fully Pressurised Gas Ships (Chlorine)

Chlorine gas is very poisonous, and the existing IMO regulations for chlorine gas ships are, therefore, extremly strict.

Fig. 2 shows a chlorine gas ship. The tanks are constructed to withstand the vapour pressure of the chlorine gas up to a temperature of +450C, which corresponds to 13,5 bar. The minimum cargo temperature is _340C. The ship has a so-called indirect cooling system, which means that cooled-down ethanol circulates in coils which are fitted to the exterior of the cargo tanks. The ethanol is cooled down in a heat exchanger with the help of evaporating freon (R22).

The tanks are insulated with polystyrene or polyurethane. Because the tanks are constructed as fully pressurised tanks, there is no need for a secondary barrier. Discharging with pumps is not permitted due to the danger of a leakage of poisonous gas. The discharging is, therefore, carried out with the help of a pressurised vapour, which could be, for example, dry nitrogen gas.

In contrast to LNG, LPG, LEG and ammonia, chlorine gas is not explosive.

Semi-Pressurised Gas Ships (LPG)

The first semi-pressurised ship was delivered in 1959. The first ships could not carry fully refrigerated cargoes, (i.e. liquid gas at atmospheric pressure). In later years, however, semi- pressurised ships have most often been built as a combination between semi-pressurised and atmospheric pressure ships. This type of vessel (fig. 3) is built in sizes from 2000m3 to approx. 24000m3, and is the most wide-spread of all LPG ships. Common cargos are LPG, LEG and NH3, along with a number of other gases..

The tanks are designed for an internal excess pressure of between 4 and 8 bar, and the minimum transport temperature is normally _330C for fully refrigerated NH3, _480C for fully refrigerated propane, or _1040C for fully refrigerated ethylene. The tanks are constructed to be self-supporting like the pressure tanks. Special steel which can stand low temperatures without becoming brittle is used as tank material. The exterior stresses and strains which the tanks are exposed to due to the thermal movements of the pipe lines on deck and the ships movement in the sea, must be reduced as effectively as possible. All the pipe lines near the tank connections must be arranged with expansion pipe bends or expansion bellows.

The most usual tank form is that of the cylinder, either single or double (as a "twin cylinder"). There are also semi-pressurised ships with spherical tanks. The cargo tanks can be placed both above and below deck, and they are considerably larger than those on a fully pressurised ship. Tanks may be arranged fore and aft or athwartships.

The tanks are stored in two cradle-like supports. The supports are made out of a heat insulating material, for example azobe wood which is a special type of hard wood. The tanks are keyed to one support, whilst on the other supports they are free to move to permit expansion and contraction.

The tanks are isolated to reduce heat transfer into the cargo. Normally foaming materials such as polyurethane or polystyrene are used with an insulation thickness of between 80 and 200 millimetres. The insulation is applied either in the form of sheets or as foam sprayed directly onto the outside of the tanks.

To reduce the risk of damage to the tanks during a collision, it is legally required that there is a minimum distance between the cargo tanks and the hull. This distance increases with the increasing levels of danger regarding the cargo itself.

Semi-pressurised ships have their own reliquefaction plant on board. This plant keeps the cargo cooled and maintains a low tank pressure, which in turn permits a reduction in material thickness in the pressure tanks. The vapour from the gas (boil-off) is extracted from the tanks using a compressor, and is then compressed and condensed in a condensor. The condensed liquid is fed back into the tanks so that there is no loss of the cargo on board the vessel. The reliquefication plant is arranged in its own compartment on deck.

For discharging the cargo, "deepwell pumps" are used. The deepwell pump consists of a multi-stage centrifugal pump placed in the bottom of the tank, a driving axle and a riser-pipe which is lead up and out of the tank to the driving motor. The driving motor is normally of an electric type. Each tank has one pump, and on deck there are normally two booster pumps. If there is a pump break down, pressure discharging is used, as it is for fully pressurised ships. In connection with each tank there are instruments for measuring the pressure, temperature and liquid level of the cargo.

Pipe arrangements on deck for a semi-pressurised ship are much more complicated than those on a fully pressurised ship. Besides the fact that they have a cooling system on board, they also have heating equipment for warming cargoes at sea and during discharge. The tanks are often segregated into two or more tank systems so that the ships can carry several cargoes simultaneously. In addition, modern semi-pressurised ships are capable of carrying solvents, since their tank construction is designed for gases with high densities.

Semi-pressurised ships also have their advantages and disadvantages:

Advantages:

Tank construction is lighter than for a fully pressurised ship and is, therefore, cheaper to build. They also have a better deadweight/displacement-ratio than fully pressurised ships.

They have increased cargo capacity due to the higher density at lower transport temperature than fully pressurised ships.

They can carry a larger number of varied cargoes than fully pressurised ships.

They have a better (more efficient) use of the hull than fully pressurised ships.

Secondary barriers for the tanks are not needed as they are for an atmospheric pressure ship.

Disadvantages:

A reliquefication plant is needed to maintain a relatively low vapour pressure in the tanks.

Economic cargo capacity is limited to approx. l5000m3 in comparison to fully pressurised ships.

The tanks must be made from expensive materials (minimum 2% nickel steel) in comparison to those used for fully pressurised ships.

Insulation of the tanks is necessary in contrast to fully pressurised ships.

Semi-pressurised Ships (Combined gas/chemical)

A type which has, in the later years, received a larger amount of interest is the combined gas and chemical ships (see fig. 4). These ships are built in accordance with the IMO's Gas and Chemicals Code, and during construction of such ships the strictest of these codes must be observed.

Combined gas and chemical ships are usually built in sizes ranging from 5000 to 8000 m3, with cylindrical tanks. The tanks are constructed for an excess pressure of 3 to 4 bar, and are built using acid-proof steel with regard to the transportation of chemicals.

As gas ships, they are built as semi-pressurised ships for the transport of LEG, LPG and ammonia. In addition to the ordinary (direct) cooling system for the cargo, they also have an indirect cooling and heating system, where there are coils fitted onto the outside of the tanks. In these coils a cooling/heating medium circulates. The indirect cooling system is used for gases where, for example, one wishes to avoid compression of the gas. (Some gases, such as butadiene, can begin to polymerise under heat supply/compression.) Another advantage with indirect cooling is that the tanks can be pre-cooled before any cargo is taken on board, and tanks heated during gas freeing.

Atmospheric Pressure Gas Ships (LPG)

Atmospheric pressure ships which were earlier called "fully refrigerated ships", are a futher development of the semi-pressurised ships. The first atmospheric pressure ship for the transporting of LPG was introduced in 1961. These ships are built in sizes from 15000m3, and upwards to 100000m3. The most common cargoes are LPG, ammonia and some other gases, that is to say mainly the same types of cargo as a semi-pressurised ship for LPG.

The cargo tanks are normally designed to withstand an internal excess pressure of approx 0,28 bar. The minimum cargo temperature is normally _500C. Due to low tank pressure the cargo tanks can be built more-or-less according to the shape of the hull (fig. 5).

The tanks are built as free standing, self supporting tanks. The fact that the tanks are self supporting means that they are not dependent on the strength of the hull other than at the tank supports. The tank material is a low temperature carbon-manganese-steel or a low temperature nickel steel with a minimum of 2% nickel.

The tanks are stored on the double bottom on wooden chocks of azobe wood, and they are supported horizontally and vertically with wooden chocks. The tanks are keyed to the centre chocks, but are free to expand or contract on the other supports.

The cargo tanks are insulated using foam materials to reduce heat leaking into the cargo. Classification societies require a secondary barrier (secondary tank) outside the cargo tank itself. This can be solved in various ways, but normally the side of the ship and the top of the double bottom tanks are made from a low temperature material. In the area around each tank there is placed an ejector, which is used during a leak, to pump the cargo over into another tank.

Each cargo tank has two discharge pumps. The pumps are powered by using an electric motor. Each tank is equipped for the measuring of pressure, temperature and liquid level etc.

The atmospheric pressure ships are equipped with reliquefication plants in the same way as semi-pressurised ships are. All of the condensed cargo is fed back into the tank, and there is, therefore, no loss of the cargo during transport. The reliquefication plant is housed on the deck as it is on semi-pressurised ships.

Atmospheric pressure ships are not as flexible as semi-pressurised ships for many reasons, one of them being that during loading and un-loading, the cargo has to be at boiling point at atmospheric pressure. These ships are built, first and foremost, with tanks for the transportation of large quantities of a single cargo, for example propane, butane or ammonia, to name the most common cargoes.

In comparison to fully pressurised ships and semi-pressurised ships, the atmospheric pressure ships have the following advantages and disadvantages:

Advantages:

The rectangular or prismatic tanks utilize the hull efficiently.

At a lower transport temperature the liquid density increases, and this leads to an increase in the cargo capacity (increased mass).

The exposed surface effect is reduced because the liquid surface reaches all the way up into the dome of the tank. It is also required that there is a liquid-proof central bulkhead in the tanks.

Larger ships are more cost efficient than smaller ships, and the atmospheric pressure ships are built in sizes up to 100000m3.

Disadvantages:

Expensive low-temperature materials have to be used for the tanks, valves and pipes.

The cargo tanks and the cargo pipe lines have to be insulated to reduce the amount of heat leaking into the cargo.

Secondary barriers are required.

The reliquefication plant has to be used to maintain the low temperature in the tanks. This plant has to have a larger reserve capacity than those for semi-pressurised ships.

An inert atmosphere is required around the tanks (removal of air and moisture).

Atmospheric pressure ships are not as flexible as semi-pressurised ships.

Atmospheric Pressure Gas Ships (LNG)

LNG, or natural gas, is always transported fully refrigerated, that is to say at a temperature of _1600C and at about atmospheric pressure. Even though in the USA they had been transporting LNG in special barges for many years, it was in 1959 that the first overseas transport of LNG was carried out. Then the carrier LNG/C "Methane Pioneer" transported 4000m3 LNG from the USA to Great Britain. Since this happened there has been a large development in both the design and size of the LNG ships.

The LNG ships are normally constructed for a maximum tank pressure of 0,25 bars, and a minimum cargo temperature of _1630C. The vessels are today built up to the size of 125000m3 (see fig. 6), but there are projects for ships up to the size of 300000m3 which have yet to be built.

Due to heat leaking into the tanks some of the cargo will evaporate. This vapour must be removed so that the tank pressure does not rise. In principle there are three different methods that can be used:

1. To eject the vapour into the atmosphere

2. To use the vapour as fuel on board

3. To condense the vapour and return it to the tank.

The first LNG ship, "Methane Pioneer", used the first method and ejected the vapour into the atmosphere, but in these days the vapour is used as fuel for the propulsion machinery together with ordinary fuel. Steam turbines are usually used for propulsion machinery, but there are in existence LNG ships with "dual-fuel" diesel motors, and also some with gas turbines. The "boil off" must not be more than can be burnt up as propulsion energy. On an LNG ship of capacity 125000m3, the "boil off" is in the region of 0,18% to 0,25% of the cargo capacity per day. This means that approx. 70% to 75% of the energy needed at full speed is covered using the gas.

Before loading, the LNG tanks are cooled down by using spray (LNG), in order to avoid too large thermal stresses. The cooling down process is carried out according to certain time/temperature criteria, which vary from one type of tank to another.

The discharge is carried out using submerged centrifugal pumps. The motor and the pump are arranged in one compact unit, which is placed on the bottom of the tanks.

It should be mentioned that the medium and small LNG ships are often built with a reliquefication plant for LEG and LPG so that the ships are more flexible. There have also been projects involving ships with reliquefication plants for LNG, but it has not been found costefficient to install such expensive and "energy eating" systems. As an example, a condensing system on a 125000m3 LNG tanker would need approx. 6000 kW of energy (8160 hK)..

Cargo Containment Systems

There are many types of tank systems for the transporting of condensed gases when one takes into account the following:

1. Maximum working pressure

2. Maximum cargo temperature

The International Maritime Organisation (IMO) divides the tanks into four main groups:

1. Integrated tanks

2. Membrane tanks

3. Semi-membrane tanks

4. Independant tanks, types A, B and C

Integrated Tanks

Here the tanks are incorporated into the hull and are exposed to the same stresses.

As normal ship's steel has a lower temperature limit of _10_C, this type of tank has no place in the transportation of condensed gases.

Membrane Tanks

Membrane tanks (fig. 7) are tanks which are not self supporting and consist of a thin membrane which rests against insulation. The insulation lies against the hull, so that the tank pressure is transferred over to this. The hull is, therefore, the element of strength in this type of construction. The most commonly used of this type is the French Gaz-Transport design. To increase safety, this class of tank requires a secondary barrier. This is solved by placing an extra membrane inside the insulation itself.

Seen from the side of the ship towards the cargo, the order of construction is as follows:

1. Side of the ship

2. Secondary insulation

3. Secondary tank

4. Primary insulation

5. Primary tank

6. Cargo

The thickness of the membrane is only 0,5mm and is made from Invarsteel (36% nickel) which has a thermal co-efficient of expansion close to nil. The problem of shrinking during the cooling of the tank is, therefore, as good as eliminated. Perolite, which is used for insulation, is a loose, corn-like material which consists of small air filled glass balls.

Semi-membrane Tanks

Semi-membrane tanks are not self-supporting in their loaded conditions and are, therefore, partly supported by the insulation between the hull and the tank. It is also necessary here for a secondary barrier to be placed on the inside of the insulation which is again fastened to the hull. The tanks are made from aluminium or 9% nickel steel.

Independent Tanks

Independent tanks are self-supporting tanks which are only keyed to the hull at the tank supports, and they are free to expand independently from the hull.

We divide independent tanks into three groups:

1. Independent tanks type A, are prismatic tanks which form the shape of the hull
(fig. 8)..

To maintain stability, the tanks are divided by a longitudinal bulkhead with an opening in the "dome" so that the vapour phases in the two sections are connected.

In the bottom of the bulkhead there is a bulkhead valve so that the pump on one side can act as a reserve for the other.

The tanks are supported on hard wood chocks on the top of the double bottom tank top, and they are held in position with tank supports both vertically and horizontally. The tanks are keyed to the tank top in the centre, but they can otherwise expand freely. Here a secondary tank is also required. On LPG ships (_550C) this is solved by using low-temperature steel in the cargo-tank area.

The English Conch-design uses this type of tank for the transport of LNG (_163_C). The tanks are built using aluminium or 9% nickel steel which are isolated with a combination of polyurethane foam, glass fibre and balsa wood. The secondary tanks are put in as a membrane in the insulation to stop the cargo coming in direct contact with the hull during a possible leak.

2. Independent tanks type B, can be the same as type A, but it should be noted that they are even safer than type A. There are stricter conditions applied to the analysing of the stresses of the material as well as the likelihood of any wear and tear on the material during ageing and the formation of cracks.

To have a tank recognised as type B, there is usually a demand for a partial secondary tank.

The normal working pressure for one of these tanks is 0,25kp/cm2 (atmospheric tanks), but they also recognise pressures up to 0,7kp/cm2.

Moss-Rosenberg spherical tank design for the transport of LNG is of type B. This is a self-supporting spherical tank which is supported in a cylindrical skirt. It is built using aluminium or 9% nickel steel.

Inside the tank there is a cylindrical tower where, among other things, all the pipe lines are collected. The tank and parts of the skirt are insulated with polystyrene.

The tanks are constructed using the "leak before failure" principle. The time it takes from when a leak is discovered to when it becomes critical is more than one year under conditions which correspond to constant storms in the North Sea.

Because the tanks are built with such high safety standards, there is no need for secondary barriers. Under each tank there is only a defined, insulated area in which to collect small leaks (fig. 9).

Fig. 8

3. Independent tanks, type C, are built as pressure tanks. They have such a large degree of safety that secondary barriers are not required. The tanks are usually cylindrical, either as single, or twin tanks.

The tanks are stored using two supports arranged as cradles, made from azobe wood, for example. They are keyed to one support, while the other supports are gliding supports which allow shrinkage during cooling.

The tanks can be used both as fully pressurised and semi-pressurised tanks.

Fully Pressurised Tanks

Today, fully pressurised tanks are seldom used for overseas transportation, but a number of small gas carriers are equipped like this, especially in Far East countries, and used in coastal trades.

The tanks are usually built for a pressure of 17,5kg/cm3 which corresponds to the boiling point of +50_C for pure propane. When the cargo is being transported at ambient temperature the cargo tank does not need to be insulated.

Semi-pressurised Tanks

Semi-pressurised tanks are normally larger than fully pressurised tanks, and are built for a working pressure of 4-8kg/cm2, but they can, as a rule, also transport fully refrigerated cargoes, for example propane (_480C) or ethylene (_1040C).

The tanks are insulated, and the cargo is cooled with the help of a reliquefication plant. In comparison to fully pressurised tanks, semi-pressurised tanks have the advantage that, due to the lower transport temperature, more cargo can be carried in the same tank volume than that of a fully pressurised tank (larger density).

Tank Materials

The various groups of cargo have specific needs for tank materials; especially important is the resistance of the materials which become brittle at low temperatures.

For cargo temperatures down to _550C (LPG) it is common to use a Carbon-Manganese fine grain steel, and for temperatures down to _1050C (ethylene) nickel steel is used.

For temperatures down to _1650C (LNG) aluminium or 9% nickel steel is used. Aluminium has the advantage that it does not lose its toughness even at very low temperatures.

Membrane tanks are made from stainless steel plates which are corrugated to allow for thermal expansions and contractions. Membrane tanks can also be made using "Invar steel", which has a very low co-efficient of thermal expansion.

Building Methods

During the planning of building methods one has to take account of the following:

Internal pressure
External pressure
Dynamic forces (due to the ships movements)
Thermal tensions
Sloshing effects
Forces (due to the ships deflections)
Reaction forces from the elements (due to the tank and the weight of the cargo)
Weight of the insulation
Weight of the equipment in the tanks
Forces due to the buoyancy of the tanks if the cargo room (void space) is filled with water
Distance between the tanks and the hull

Insulation

For the transport of cargoes with lower temperatures than _100C, the tanks must be insulated so that the hull is not exposed to such low temperatures.

The main function of the insulation is to reduce the amount of heat leaking into the cargo so that the "boiling off" and the rising pressure of the cargo is kept to a minimum.

The most common insulation materials are:

1. Polyurethane

2. Polystyrene

3. Perlite

4. Balsa wood

There are many methods and mediums for tank insulation. For self-supporting tanks polyurethane or polystyrene are usually used because they are just glued directly onto the tanks. The insulation is covered on the outside with a moisture barrier.

Another method is to glue sheets of insulation onto the outside of the tanks in a "square mesh" formation, then a shield of galvanised steel is erected on the outside of the tank, while the gap in between that and the tank is filled with polyurethane foam.

For spherical tanks, a spinning method is used where a specially constructed arm drives round and round the tank and lays (spins) insulation onto the outside.

There are also tank designs where the insulation is placed on the inside of the hull plating.

Answers to the Exercises

Answers to the exercises for page 7

1. By condensed gas we mean a gas (vapour) which is made into a liquid form (liquefied) using pressure or temperature (or both).

2. It is mainly propane and butane which are regarded as LPG cargoes, but propylene, iso-butane and butylene, along with a mixture of these gases, are also considered as LPG cargoes.

3. NLG (Natural Liquid Gases) or wet gases are a mixture of mainly ethane, propane and butane, which are extracted during the separation of crude oil/gas.

LPG, however, is a distilled product which is produced during the destilling of crude oil and gases.

4. When we say that ethylene is a cold gas, we mean that the boiling point is low in comparison to other gases (the boiling point for ethylene at atmospheric pressure is
_1040C).

5. By the transport temperature we refer to the temperature that a determined cargo is transported at.

6. When the temperature of a gas cargo increases, the tank pressure will rise. The temperature and pressure are proportional to one another (when we know the one value, we can find the other by using a table or a curve diagram).

Answers to the exercises from page 24

1. With a fully pressurised ship we can transport, for example, propane, butane, propylene, butylene, iso-butane, ammonia and chlorine.

2. With a semi-pressurised ship we can transport, for example, propane, butane, iso-butane, propylene, butylene, ammonia and ethylene. On certain semi-pressurised ships (with indirect cooling systems), we can carry special cargoes such as butadiene etc.

3. Spherical tanks can be used for fully pressurised ships, semi-pressurised ships and atmospheric pressure ships (LNG).

4. It is the ambient temperature (air and sea) which decides the temperature of a cargo on board a fully pressurised ship.

5. The main difference between the transportation of chlorine gas and LPG-gas, is that chlorine is highly poisonous, but it is not explosive. In contrast to LPG-gas, the chlorine gas cannot be cooled directly, and cannot be discharged using pumps.

6. By indirect cooling, we mean the cooling of a cargo without it being used itself as the cooling medium (for example, to avoid the compression of the vapour). This can be arranged by having cooled ethanol circulating in coils around the outside of the cargo tanks.

7. By a "fully refrigerated ship" we really mean an "atmospheric pressure ship". In other words, a ship which transports a cargo at atmospheric pressure (or near atmospheric pressure). That means the cargo has to be cooled down (fully refrigerated) so that the saturated pressure of the cargo is about the same as the atmospheric pressure.

8. The most flexible of the gas ships is the semi-pressurised ship. These ships can transport more or less the same cargoes as atmospheric pressured or fully pressurised ships along with a few more (ethylene, ethane etc.). In addition, the semi-pressurised ships can load and discharge at high temperatures and pressure variations for the same type of cargo.

9. In the void space around the tanks there is placed an ejector, which, if there is a leak from a tank into the void space, can be used to pump the cargo over into another tank (the ejector uses the cargo as a fuel).

10. The reason that the atmosphere around the tanks on an atmospheric pressure ship has to be inerted, is to remove the oxygen (air) from the surroundings to reduce the risk of fire or explosion. Also, when the atmosphere is replaced with dry inert gas instead of air, the corrosion and condensation problems are reduced.

Answers to the exercises from page 34

1. By the expression "integrated tank", we mean a cargo tank which is part of the hull (like a cargo tank on board a normal oil tanker).

2. The approximate tank pressure for a cargo of propane at +500C is 17,5 bar.

3. The tank materials which are used for cargo temperatures down to _10_C, _104_C and _165_C are respectively: normal ship's steel (carbon steel), 5% nickel steel and 9% nickel steel (or aluminium).

4. The most important job of the insulation is to reduce the amount of heat leaking into the cargo, as well as protecting the hull from being exposed to the extremely low temperatures of the cargo.

5. A gas tank is made in such a way that it can expand and contract without causing possible damage to the ship or the tank itself. The tanks can be stored on hard wood chocks on top of the double bottom, and supported vertically and horizontally with cradles. In the middle, the tanks can be keyed to the double bottom tank top like the prismatic "independent tank - type A".
