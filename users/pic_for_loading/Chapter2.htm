Organic and Inorganic Chemistry

Inorganic chemistry deals with all chemistry except that for the carbon atom. Besides carbon, the elements consist of 102 other known materials. These are arranged in the periodic table (see fig. 2).

The periodic table is built on the principle that the electrons in the outer shell determine the chemical properties of a material.

An atom consists of protons, electrons and neutrons (fig. 1), and can have a maximum of 8 electrons in the outer electron shell. There are just as many protons as there are electrons in an atomic element. Meanwhile, the number of neutrons can vary.

Fig.1
Atomic model of hydrogen and helium

In the periodic table the elements are arranged according to how many protons and electrons they have. The vertical columns in the periodic table are called groups. There are 8 main groups and 8 lesser groups. In the main groups the number will give the number of electrons the element has in its outer shell (Valence electrons).

The periods are the horizontal rows. They indicate which electron shell is being filled. As there are 7 electron shells, there are 7 periods accordingly. The electron shell can also be regarded as the energy level for the electrons.

Elements in group IA have a tendency to emit one electron. The elements in group VIIA, however, have a tendency to be receptive to one electron. Therefore, the elements from groups IA and VIIA have a tendency to react together. With the emitting and receiving of electrons, ions are formed and we get an ion-bond.

Example: Na + Cl _ Na+ + Cl_ _ NaCl

The chlorine (Cl) has received one electron from the sodium (Na) and it has been electrically charged. The chlorine atom has, in other words, undergone a chemical reaction with the sodium, and they have combined to form a product we call cooking salt. Elements in the groups IA and VIIA have a tendency to form ionic bonds with each other. When two or more atoms combine together, we call the product a molecule. NaCl (sodium-chloride or cooking salt) is a molecule.

When two atoms have one or more pairs of electrons which they share, it is called a covalent bond. Covalent bonds are common in both organic and inorganic reactions.

The main part of the inorganic elements are metals. They form metallic bonds where the atoms are packed closely together. All the way to the right of the periodic table we find the noble gases. Noble gases occur only in atomic form.

Unsaturated Bonding

Bonds in organic chemistry which contain a double or a triple bond between two or more carbon atoms are called unsaturated bonds. Characteristics of these unsaturated bonds are that they can have their valencies saturated (spare pairs of electrons) by treating them with elements with quite high activities, for example, halogen. In the laboratory, a solution of bromine and water is used to show such double bonds. If we shake the unsaturated bondings in some of this bromine water, the brown colour of the bromine will eventually disappear. The bromine reacts when the double bonds open and bromine atoms attach to each spare electron, the valencies being, therefore, filled. The reaction occurs as below:

Peroxides

One particular type of catalyst which is used to a great extent in the production of plastics is peroxides. As the name suggests the peroxide contains two oxygen atoms bonded together, for example benzol peroxide:

Also hydrogen peroxide H-O-O-H. The peroxides decompose easily into radicals, eg. benzol peroxide..

All peroxides are highly explosive and must be handled with the utmost care. If the peroxides are kept damp, then they will not explode.

Peroxides can form layers as a powder in pipes, containers and tanks. The materials are very unstable and can decompose with great force and explode, especially when they are found in concentrated amounts and are exposed to heat or jolts. The lightest hammer blow can set off an explosion.

To prevent the formation of peroxides with unsaturated bondings such as in 1, 3-butadiene, ethene, VCM etc, it is necessary to have as low an oxygen content as possible in the pipes and tanks. We have seen that peroxides are used as polymerisation catalysts, and we know that most of the unsaturated bondings themselves form peroxides with the oxygen. There is often set a max. limit (before loading) of 0,3 vol% of O2 in the atmosphere of the tank. The products themselves have a limit of around 5 vol. ppm O2

Inhibitors

To get rid of the last traces of oxygen, one uses materials which, even in low concentrations, react freely with oxygen. These materials are called inhibitors. Another property of some of these materials is that they can react with radicals so that the speed of polymerisation can be slowed down or stopped.

During transport by sea of 13-butadiene to the USA, the US Coast Guard demands that the cargo is inhibited with 100 ppm of TBC (tertiary butyl catechol). This is to hinder any polymerisation and developments of strong heat. TBC hinders the formation of peroxides. VCM, which is transported over several days, has added about 5 ppm of hydroquinon to prevent any polymerisation of the cargo into PVC.

Most of the inhibitors are highly poisonous and dangerous to our health, and must, therefore, be handled with the utmost care.

Stench Agents

Due to many of the gases being highly flammable and explosive, it is very important that any small leaks can be discovered as soon as possible. Therefore, in some cases a material is added to the LPG, for example mercaptane. These materials have a very intense smell (like rotten eggs) even in very low concentrations such as only a few ppm (grams/tons) -

Mixing of Temperatures

Heat always travels from a warm material or substance to a colder material - from a higher temperature to a lower temperature.

If we assume that there are two substances (or materials) which are mixed or in a solution, and there are no chemical reactions occurring, we can say that the heat which is given off is equal to the heat received. We have, therfore, made another assumption; that is, that there occurs no heat exchange with the surroundings.

The heat which one material gives off is equal to the heat which the other material receives. The heat exchange goes on until the temperatures of both the materials are equal.

This is a form of reasoning which is directly applicable when you are going to transport cargoes with different temperatures and specific heat capacities (eg - propane and butane), and these cargoes are to be mixed on board. In this way you can calculate beforehand the temperature of the mixed cargoes before you mix them together. Aggregate Conditions

By experience, we know that materials can exist in different states, or as we say: aggregate conditions. The aggregate conditions which we know from day-to-day experience are solids, liquids and vapours, but in addition there exists a fourth phase which is called plasma (solid (s), liquid (l), gas (g) and plasma).

The condition is dependent on temperature and pressure. Any element could go through all three (four) phases if we varied the pressure and temperature.

We say that a substance sublimes if it transforms directly from solid to gas (ie, does not become liquid).

When we heat up a solid, we can read off the melting point and boiling point as we proceed. The temperature keeps itself constant until all the solid is melted, after which the temperature rises again until the liquid begins to boil. The temperature remains constant until all of the liquid has evaporated. See figure 9.

Solids

Solids have fixed volumes and fixed forms. A solid is built up as a "network". That means that the atoms or molecules have set distances and set positions in relation to each other. When many of the same type of "network" join together, the material formed has a regular shape which we call crystalline. The shaping of the "network" decides, for example, how hard a material is or how it will conduct heat or electricity.

Liquids

Liquids. have fixed volumes, but no fixed shape. A liquid can, therefore, only be held in a container. The exposed surface is horizontal when the liquid is undisturbed.

In a liquid the distance between the molecules is small, and the bonds between them are no larger than to allow them to move freely about one another. The molecules are so tightly packed that the liquid barely changes its volume when compressed.

Vapours

In a vapour there are very weak bonds between the separate particles. Vapours are independent of form and size, even in a container.

The molecules in a vapour are in constant motion. They collide constantly with each other, also with the container they are in. This colliding against the walls produces a pressure, the vapour pressure. This is called Brownian motion after the name of its discoverer, Robert Brown. These movements become faster as the gas is heated up. Therefore, the pressure of the gas increases as the heat increases. Reversing the theory, the gas pressure will become lower as the temperature drops and the movement of the particles slows down.

Phase Changes

Melting and evaporation are both phase changes. We use the same description with freezing and condensing. During phase changes there is always energy either used or given off. For a substance to either melt or evaporate energy must be added. The internal energy in the material rises, but the temperature of the material remains the same.

If we have a piece of ice (see fig. 9) at _40C and we transfer heat to it, the temperature will rise from _400C to 00C. If we continue to supply heat, the ice will melt and will turn to water without the temperature continuing to rise. If we now continue to apply heat, the temperature in the water will rise to 100C. At this temperature the water will begin to boil and evaporate without the temperature rising any more. The temperature of the steam begins to rise only when all of the water has evaporated.

Melting

When a solid melts, the atoms are torn loose from their positions in the "network" and slide about each other. Energy is needed to break the "network". The required energy does not necessarily have to be in the form of heat. It can be mechanical energy, for example, rubbing ice cubes together, although the energy used is usually heat. This heat is called the melting heat. The energy transferred to the solid, which is transferred under a constant temperature, does not disappear, but is used to increase the internal potential energy of the solid. We get it back when the material solidifies again.

Careful experimenting has revealed that a mixture of solid and liquid phases of the same substance always has the same temperature when the pressure over the mixture is the same. This temperature, where it is possible to have a mixture of solid and liquid phases of one substance, is the melting point of the substance at that pressure. The melting point is the same as the freezing point.

Evaporation

If we let a liquid stand in a container in the open air, we know that the liquid will disappear after a while. If we hang out wet clothes to dry in the winter during sub-zero temperatures, we know that they will dry if they hang long enough in dry weather. We can find many examples of water drying. This must signify that the liquid changes to vapour. We call this evaporation. Perhaps we think mainly of evaporation in connection with boiling, but we had best note that evaporation can occur at all temperatures. Some liquids evaporate quickly, like ethane and benzene. And others evaporate slowly, like oil.

As a rule things dry quicker, the warmer they are, but there are exceptions. In the tropics the air is so humid that drying clothes is often difficult or impossible.

Evaporation depends on the thermal movements of the molecules.


We can explain evaporation in the following way: in a liquid the molecules are in constant motion, but all the molecules do not travel at the same speed. The speed of the molecules in liquids varies in the same way as it does in gas (Vapour). Some of the molecules at the surface of the gas have enough speed and, therefore, enough kinetic energy to allow them to escape from their bonds in the liquid and become a vapour.

This effect occurs because the liquid uses the heat (energy) from the skin to evaporate.

Condensing

All forms of vapour turn back into liquid if the vapour is cooled at a certain pressure. We call this condensing.

Condensing can occur at all temperatures, as the case is for evaporation.

We can explain this by using the kinetic theory of gases. There are weak bonds between the gas molecules, and by cooling the gas their speed decreases, and, therefore, so does their kinetic energy. The collisions between the molecules become less violent. This causes a number of molecules to join together, and as a result a liquid begins to form. During condensing the gas gives off its evaporation heat which was used to transform it into a gas, and its internal energy decreases.

Saturated and Unsaturated Vapour

If we consider a container with an air tight piston, and we fill a little water into it, or even better a liquid which evaporates easily, we let the piston rest a distance up from the liquid (fig. 11). After a while an amount of the liquid has evaporated, the process goes quicker the higher the temperature is. The molecules in the liquid move randomly. The molecules which are moving at great speed in the liquid will eventually escape and become vapour. But some of the vapour molecules will also hit the surface of the liquid and enter it again. After a while there will be so many vapour molecules in the area over the liquid that there will be equal numbers of molecules becoming vapour as there are numbers becoming liquid. We can now say that the area over the liquid is saturated with vapour. If we move the piston up a little higher, so that the volume above the liquid increases, we will see that a larger amount of the liquid evaporates, and the pressure will, after a while, be constant again. The assumption for this is that the temperature is held constant.

If the piston is pressed down, the volume over the liquid, therefore, becomes smaller, some of the vapour will condense to liquid, but again the pressure after a little while will be constant.

We can explain the experiment as follows:

When the liquid is in an enclosed space the molecules which evaporate will remain inside the space. At the same time the opposite occurs with the vapour molecules hitting the surface of the liquid and becoming liquid again. Gradually, as there is more vapour above the liquid, more molecules will hit the surface of the liquid.

After a while, just as many molecules come out of the liquid as there are going back into the liquid. We can now say that the vapour and the liquid are stable (in equilibrium). Now the vapour has a certain pressure which is dependent on the temperature of the liquid. This is called the saturation pressure. As long as there is liquid in the container the pressure of the vapour is independent of the volume. The pressure is the same as the saturation pressure. When we increase the volume, then more of the liquid will evaporate into vapour. If the volume decreases, then the vapour will turn into a liquid. The vapour pressure is always the same as the saturation pressure. If we increase the temperature, more molecules escape from the liquid and become vapour, and the density and pressure of the vapour becomes higher.

The vapour pressure and temperature are very closely related. The pressure of a vapour at a certain temperature cannot be higher than the saturation pressure at that temperature. On the other hand the opposite can occur if there is not enough liquid to fill the container with vapour at the saturation temperature. In this case all the liquid is evaporated, and the vapour will have a lower pressure than the saturated vapour would have at this temperature. We say, therefore, that the vapour is unsaturated or super-heated.

Figure 12 shows the saturation temperature for propane, butane and water at different temperatures. The normal boiling point can be read off at the saturation pressure at 1 bar.

The saturation pressure increases with the temperature, and at the same temperature the pressure is highest for propane and is lowest for water. The difference in the saturation pressures is caused by the bonds between the molecules. The inter-molecular strength of the bonds is largest for water and smallest for propane. We are really talking about how volatile the liquids are.

The boiling point of a liquid is at the temperature where the saturation pressure of the liquid is the same as the pressure over the liquid. We can see from fig. 12 that a liquid with a high saturation pressure has a low boiling point.

A saturated vapour will increase its density as the temperature rises, as long as the vapour and the liquid are kept in a closed system (container).

The density for saturated vapour and the saturation pressure can be read off curves or tables for each gas. This will be explored more in the next letter.

Boiling

Everyone has seen water boil in a pan on a hot plate, and has seen that vapour bubbles from inside the liquid. The pressure inside the bubble must be larger than the sum of the pressure from the surface tension inside the bubble and the pressure from the surrounding liquid. If we consider a vapour bubble formed at the bottom of the container (fig. 13) the pressure (p) inside the bubble must be the same as the barometer pressure (pb) plus the static pressure (_gh):

P = pb + _gh

These bubbles must be saturated with water vapour. When the saturation pressure in the bubbles at the surface is a little larger than the pressure over the water, then the water boils. The temperature of the vapour just above the water is the same as the boiling point of the liquid at the pressure in the room.

We can define: The boiling point of a liquid as the temperature at which the saturation pressure of the vapour is the same as the pressure above the liquid.

If the pressure above the liquid is the same as the atmospheric pressure, then the boiling point is the temperature where the saturation pressure is the same as the atmospheric pressure.

If the pressure above a liquid decreases, the boiling point will decrease. In reverse, if the pressure above a liquid is increased, the liquid boils at a higher temperature.

In a steam production plant we know this from practice. In a modern boiler water boils at, for example, 65bar at 2810C. During fresh water production in a fresh water generator it can boil at a temperature of 400C corresponding to a saturation pressure of 0,0738bar.

At 5000m above sea level the air pressure is approx. 5l3mbar, here the boiling point of water is 820C.

Distillation

If we heat up a mixture of two liquids, both components are then present in the vapour. The saturation pressure of such a mixture is always lower than the sum of the vapour pressure of the components, and the boiling point of the mixture generally has a mean value.

If we condense this vapour, in the beginning we will mostly get the volatile liquids, then more and more of the less volatile ones, and at the end we will mainly get the pure component. This heating and condensing is called distillation. (See fig. 14)

The properties of gases can be explained with help from the kinetic theory of gases. (Kinetic is Greek, and means movement).

A gas consists of a large number of uncharged atoms or molecules. Single atoms like: He, Ne and Ar (Noble gases) do not bond together, but continue to be separate atoms.

Nitrogen, hydrogen and oxygen will bond together, so that two and two atoms form molecules: N2, H2 and O2 A larger number of atoms of the same kind can also form molecules. Four phosphor atoms form, for example, the molecule P4.

The molecules in a vapour (also in a liquid) are in constant motion, the so-called Brownian motion, which we have spoken of earlier.

The movements are completely coincidental and random, that is to say that a particular molecule, over a period of time, could arrive anywhere in a room.

The speed of the molecule is dependent on the temperature. The higher the temperature, the faster they move.

The speed of diffusion for a gas is also dependent on the weight of the molecule. The British scientist Graham discovered this in 1829 and he deduced the law:

The diffusion speed of a gas is inversely proportional to the square root of the molecular mass of the gas.

The Solubility of Gases in Liquids

Liquids can absorb gases (absorb is Latin for to suck in). The gas is dissolved into the liquid (CO2 dissolved in water is soda!).

Example:

Henry's Law states that: "The absorbed mass of gas is proportional to the gas pressure over the liquid".

This law only works when no chemical reactions are taking place, as, for example, with ammonia and water or hydrogen chloride and water. The absorbed mass of gas is also dependent on temperature. The higher the temperature, the less gas is absorbed. (Fish die in rivers and lakes due to high temperatures which leads to little O2 being dissolved in the water).

Reactivity and Corrosion

Natrium metal reacts violently with water. Chlorine gas reacts violently with copper. Nitrogen does not react with oxygen at room temperature etc. The reason for this is called "chemical affinity".

Natrium has a great affinity to water, and chlorine gas has a great affinty to copper etc.

Atomic theory states that the electrons of the atoms decide the chemical properties of a substance.

In a steam boiler where the water contains traces of oxygen, we know that iron oxide is formed. The oxygen has an affinity to iron.

Hydrate Formation

By analysing salts, it can be shown that many of them bond with one or more water molecules. Substances containing water molecules are called hydrates. An older term is crystal water. If we take some blue Copper sulphate and heat it up, the blue colour eventually disappears, and at the end a white liquid is formed. If we leave this to cool of in normal air, its blue colour will return.

CuSO4 5H2O (blue) CuSO4 (white) + 5H2O

The explanation of this "loose connection" between the water molecules and several chemical substances is due to the construction of the water molecule.

The water molecule consists of one oxygen atom and two hydrogen atoms with an angle between them of approx. 1040. Due to this, the electric charge of the oxygen atom is displaced (see fig. 21).

In organic chemistry there are also a number of substances which form hydrates like, for example, propane. We all know that hydrocarbons can absorb an amount of water, for example petrol, oil, benzene, VCM etc. If we cool these liquids the water is separated out. But the hydrates form a new/different substance. In the example propane-hydrate, a white ointment-like substance is formed.

To hinder the formation of hydrates we can add alcohols, like for example methanol or ethanol. This addition of alcohols or similar substances must only be used in situations where the product specifications allows this. The super clean gases like ethylene or propylene have stringent regulations regarding this. (Ethylene, max. l0vol. ppm. methanol, and propylene max. 5 vol. ppm.).

Additives have a negative effect on polymerisation. Products like LPG, ethane or propane also have limits on the content of ethanol/methanol, but these values are higher.

From thermodynamics we know that temperature is an expression for the movement of the molecules. That is to say that the molecules in a vapour are in a greater motion than in a liquid, and in least motion in a solid. This means that the viscosity in a liquid increases with decreasing temperature.

E.g.: Motor oil at +300C is runny, but the same oil at -200C is very viscous, like treacle.

During the cooling of hydrocarbons in the liquid phase we will, in some cases, get separation of water. This shows that the separability of water (and several other compounds), is dependent on the temperature. An example is diesel oil which can be completely clear at +200C, but if cooled to 00C the oil is misty.

Besides the separation of water there can also be the separation of paraffin-wax. What it is, is easily shown in a laboratory.

With the handling of condensed gases such as ethane, ethylene, propane and propylene etc., where the temperature of the liquid can be _1000C, it is clear that the material in the valves, pumps, pipes and tanks is exposed to colossal stress.

The crystal structure in the metal can be changed and it becomes brittle. During cooling, if it is too rapid, cracks can form in the metal due to the temperature change and its brittleness.

During liquid leaking at such low temperatures on exposed deck and shell plating, water hosing can be used to keep them warm and at the same time disperse the gas.

In connection with liquid leakage collected in containers (e.g. a drip tray), one must avoid using water to evaporate the liquid because there can be a danger of rupture of the container if the water freezes.

Melting of Ice

Among other things ice can create huge problems for the service of valves and the working of pumps. To hinder the formation of ice alcohols (normally methanol or ethanol) are added.

In Norway ethanol is used, mainly due to the toxicity of methanol. These alcohols are completely soluble in water, and the freezing point of the mixture will sink (see fig. 22).

To hinder external ice formation on valves, a methanol or ethanol spray is often used.
