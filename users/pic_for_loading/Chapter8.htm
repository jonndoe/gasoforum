Dew Point Measurement

 

Air

Air is the term used for the mixture of gases we find in the atmosphere. The relative mixtures of the different gases do not change with place and height, except for the water moisture content which can vary greatly. The composition of dry air is:

· Nitrogen (N2) 78,09 vol%
· Oxygen (O2) 20,93 vol%
· Argon (A) 0,93 vol%
· Carbon Dioxide (CO2) 0,03 vol%
· Others 0,02 vol%

On the Earth's surface 1 cubic meter of air weighs abt 1,3 kilo. The air is thinner at a higher altitude. At a height of 5000 m 1 cubic metre of air weighs abt 0,7 kilo, and at 10000m it weighs abt 0,4 kilo.

Water Vapour in the Air

There is always some water vapour in the air, i.e. water in the form of gas. It is as clear as air and is found in varying quantities, from unmeasurable to abt 40g/m3 which is over 3% of the mass of air. Water vapour is continually supplied from evaporation of seas, lakes and damp ground. Evaporation occurs at all temperatures (also from ice and snow), but it is most active when the air is dry and warm and the wind is blowing. In these conditions the damp air is blown away and new dry air replaces it.

Consider one cubic metre of air at a set temperature in contact with a water surface. Evaporation from the water surface will stop when the moisture content in the air has reached a certain maximum value. This value, which is given in grams per cubic metre, is the equilibrium between water and vapour. The air is saturated. The warmer the air is, the more water vapour the air can carry before being saturated.

If saturated air is warmed up, it can carry more moisture, corresponding to the temperature rise. If it is cooled, it will be super saturated. The excess water vapour will then change into small water drops or ice crystals with a diameter of abt 0,01mm which we see as fog, clouds, dew or frost.

Types of Dew Point Meters

There are several different types of dew point meters available. The simplest (and cheapest) method is to mix acetone and dry ice in a cup. When the temperature in the cup falls to a certain level, dew will form on the outside of the cup. Using a thermometer, the temperature in the cup can be measured. This temperature will then be the dew-point temperature of the air (atmosphere ).

Some other dewpoint instruments use Freon (R-22) to cool a flask. When dew forms on the flask the dew-point temperature has been reached. This is the most suitable type of dew-point meter for a gas ship.

Electronic dew-point meters are available, but they are costly and often rather inaccurate.

Common for all dew-point meters is that they measure the temperature that the air (or tank atmosphere) must be cooled down to, in order to be saturated with water vapour.

Measurement Theory

The difference between the tank-atmosphere temperature and the dew-point in the tank gives us the number of degrees that the tank atmosphere must be cooled down before water vapour is separated out as dew or mist. A large difference means that the tank atmosphere is dry. A small difference is found when the tank atmosphere is damp. At saturation the dew-point and actual temperature fall together.

In Letter No. 3 there are two diagrams and a table showing the maximum water vapour content in air at different temperatures. As you can see, cold air is able to contain less water vapour than warm air. Study the five exercise examples that are shown in the diagrams. The exercise examples together with the diagrams and tables should be self explanatory.

As a low dew-point is often required for certain cargoes, it is important to understand the theory of dew-point measuring as well as being able to use the dew-point meter correctly. Read the instruction manual before use, and learn to understand it. It is often required that the tank atmosphere has a maximum dew-point (e.g. dew-point not higher than _300C).

The dew-point meter can be used for air as well as for example inert gas, or nitrogen without any correction being required.

Absolute humidity is given in grams per cubic metre (grams of water per cubic metre of atmosphere). This is the value we measure using a dew-point meter (psychrometer) and a table or curve for the maximum water-vapour content in air.

Relative humidity is the ratio between the actual water-vapour content in the tank atmosphere and the maximum water-vapour content that the tank atmosphere can contain at that actual temperature given as a percentage. The relative humidity can be measured directly with a hygrometer.

Insulation

The transportation of heat through insulation material can occur in three ways: as heat radiation, as conduction through the solid material and as conduction through the trapped air or gas found in the insulation material.

Most insulation materials get their main insulative properties from the insulative capacity of the stationary air. The heat conduction through stationary air is about 0,024 W/m·K.

In principal, insulation material is constructed so that air is trapped in pores or air layers between fibres or in several insulation layers. It is very important that the air is kept stationary or convection will occur and heat will be transported. We try, therefore, to close the air in small spaces, or ensure that the air molecules stick to thin fibres, i.e. we use fibre materials that have such a structure that the air molecules hardly move. The heat conduction of the gas in the cell material, or the air between the fibres in a fibre material will be a considerable part of the total heat conduction through the insulation material. As long as the pore diameter is larger than the free length of track for movement of the gas molecules, but not so large that convection occurs, the value of the heat conduction for the gas will be constant.

If we change the air with a gas which has a lower heat conduction value, we have an insulation material with a lower heat conduction than with stationary air. This is the case for plastic insulation of polyurethane where air is replaced with freon gas which has a lower heat conduction value than air.

Insulation Materials

Insulation materials used on gas ships can be split into three types, in order of the hardness of the materials (see Figure 1).

1. Hard materials that can take heavy loads and can be used as tank supports. An example of such insulation materials includes several types of timber such as balsa and azobe and certain plastic foams with a relatively high density. The materials must be able to withstand the stresses that occur when tanks shrink during cooling.

2. Soft materials such as mineral wool and cell plastic with low density. The soft materials are fixed with glue and/or various clip arrangements fixed to the tanks or to other barriers.

3. Materials in powder form like perlite. When using powder materials the whole area around the tanks is filled. The material acts like a liquid and can be easily filled in the spaces between tanks and other barriers. This material has been used mainly for atmospheric-pressure ships.

Mineral Wool

Mineral wool is a joint term for different types of inorganic fibre rich insulation materials and can be divided into glass wool and rock wool. Mineral-wool insulation materials are supplied primarily as sheets and mats.

Glass-wool sheets are made of melted glass and by centrifugal rotation formed into very fine fibres (average diameter abt 0,005mm).

Rock-wool sheets are made from one of several types of rock that are melted at a temperature of abt 16000C and then by a centrifugal rotation formed to fibres, in the same way as for glass wool. Expanded Perlite

Perlite is made from a vulcanic rock type with a perlitic structure. The main constituents are abt 71% SiO2 and abt 16% AL202. The raw material contains some water (abt 3,5%) which, when heated to abt 12000C, vaporises and "blows" the material up. Expansion is 10 - 20 times with numerous closed air-tight cells. At the same time the individual grains separate from one another, the material "explodes" and forms sharp edged grains with sizes up to 3mm with great mechanical strength. The density is about 50kg/m3.

Cell Plastic

There are many different plastic insulation materials, but we are to limit ourselves to polyurethane and polystyrene.

Polyurethane

The most important components in polyurethane are isocyanat and polyol. The materials are delivered in liquid form; isocyanate is similar to thick oil and polyol similar to varnish liquid. When the liquids are mixed and water added, an exothermic chemical reaction begins. The carbon in the isocyanate combines with the hydrogen in the polyol, and CO2 gas is formed. Due to the reaction heat this gas will vaporise and expand the material. A foam is produced with abt 90% closed cells and a low heat conduction value. The lower conduction value is 0,030 - 0,045W/m2K for normal insulation material where most holes in the fibre or the pores in the cells in the material are filled with air. The heat conduction in an insulation material where the cells are filled with CO2 gas will have a corresponding value. Much better values are obtained if Freon 11 (Ru) is used as a propellant media. Freon 11 has a molecular mass of 138, which means heavier and larger molecules. At +100C Freon 11 has a heat conduction value of 0,008W/m2·K, which is abt 3 times lower than for air.

Initially after hardening, diffusion of CO2 and air will occur. A stable condition of the material with a higher heat conduction value will be reached after abt 100 days. At particularly low temperatures the gas condenses and we must reckon with a very low pressure in the cells. It can be expected that some air will gradually leak into the cell structure as this cannot withstand the extremely high pressure changes. The conditions are more favourable when the foam is sealed by watertight walls on all sides.

Instead of Ru, R12 is also used because it can be sprayed and will expand as it is applied, as the R12 vaporises easily. Such a spray method is called frothing in contrast to foaming where practically all the expansion occurs after the foam is in place (35 to 40 times expansion). The density of polyurethane foam is abt 40kg/m3.

Polystyrene

The raw material used for polystyrene insulation is a type of polystyrene that can be expanded, and it gets a cell structure with a great number of closed cells. The material contains propellants i.e. expanding gases that blow up the individual cells. The production takes place in two stages, first pre-expanding with vapour where balls or pearls with a diameter of 1 - 6mm are formed. The pre-expanded material is then placed in large siloes for airing. After having been in the silo for 2 - 4 days the pre-expanded material is filled into moulds for further expansion and moulding of the grains so that blocks are formed. Superheated steam or electrical elements are used to aid expansion.

Dampness in the Insulation

As we know atmospheric air contains water vapour in variable quantities up to a saturation point. The water vapour in the air seeps towards areas with lower temperatures, therefore a well layered diffusion tight material is needed on the warm side of the insulation of a gas tank. Even if the diffusion-tight membranes are put on the outside of the insulation, the membrane will never be 100% gas tight. Particular problems will be encountered at tank supports, dome and pipe passages to get the insulation diffusion tight. An important property for the insulation materials is their vapour diffusion properties and their water absorption capability. Insulation placed on a cold tank will eventually absorb moisture, even if it is supposed to be 100% water-vapour tight. If the temperature is below 00C, we can also get ice expansion fracturing the insulation. Particularly perlite, glass and rock wool have bad diffusion resistance, whilst other cellplastic materials such as polystyrene, polyurethane and PVC have good water vapour diffusion properties.

If diffusion resistance in still air is set at 1,0, the diffusion resistance for perlite is abt 1,2, for glass and rock wool 1,4, polystyrene and polyurethane 50 - 70 and for PVC 170.

Several methods using diffusion-tight materials (moisture barrier) have been used, for example:

1. Glassfibre strengthened polyester. This covering will easily become brittle, and a relatively large thickness must be used to obtain sufficient tensile strength.

2. One or several layers of asphalt emulsion with several layers of glass fibre matting.

3. Thin aluminium foil glued directly onto cell plastic.

Emergency Shut Down Systems

 

Description and Method of Working

For a gas ship with a maximum tank pressure under 0,7bar there is a quick closing valve system at the shore connections..

For gas ships with a tank pressure over 0,7bar there is, in addition, a quick closing valve on all vapour and liquid connections to the tanks (except for safety valves, liquid level gauges and other instruments).

The quick closing system has to be "fail-safe" i.e. all the valves have to close if pressure or voltage drops.

All quick closing valves are connected to a central release system that can be operated manually from at least two locations on board, with one being outside the cargo tank area and the other by the loading/discharging connections or in the cargo control room.

The quick closing system also has a thermal release operated by melt fuses at each cargo tank and at the loading/discharging connections. If one of the melt fuses is subject to a temperature of abt 1000C, the system is activated.

If the quick closing system (emergency shut-down system) is released, the cargo compressors and cargo pumps are also stopped.

At some shore terminals it is also required that the quick closing system can be released from the jetty side, and if desired also interconnected with the shore shut down system.

Each individual quick closing valve on the liquid lines has a regulator to adjust the closure speed. However, it can be difficult to decide on a correct closure speed, and there are often several views on this. See the following section on liquid and pressure surge in connection with closure speed.

Liquid and Pressure Surge

Liquid surge (pressure surge or "liquid hammer") can occur if valves are opened or closed too quickly. This pressure can be enough to fracture pipelines and cargo hoses. The closure speed of remote controlled valves must be controlled at regular intervals before cargo operation, and if necessary adjusted.

Liquid surge may also occur when automatic shut down valves are controlled by level alarms or by quick closing valves with too short closure time.

If the ship is loading 500m3 of propane per hour through a 8" pipeline which is 500m long, there will be 15m3 of liquid moving at a speed of 4,5m/sec. If the quick closing valve on the loading line is closed instantaneously, the liquid flow will be stopped momentarily. This will result in a violent and momentary pressure increase upstream of the valve. This pressure increase is propagated back up the pipeline as a wave ("liquid hammer") at the speed of sound.

When such a pressure wave passes equipment such as cargo loading arm/hoses, expansion bellows, valves or pumps, they may be seriously damaged. If the pressure wave hits a closed valve or something similar, it will be thrown back as an echo. This will gradually diminish in strength.. If several pressure waves occur at the same time in the pipeline system the equipment can be subject to a total pressure that is equivalent to the sum of the pressure waves.

See Figure 2 which is taken from the Tanker Safety Guide (Liquefied Gas).

If the liquid contains gas bubbles or if there are gas pockets in the system, these will condense (collapse) instantaneously during the pressure increase, and this gives a sharper pressure surge.

Downstream from the quick closing valve there will be a sudden pressure drop due to the inertia of the liquid. This will cause a momentary boiling which also gives pressure surge.

If the quick closing valve had closed slowly, the liquid flow would have gradually decreased, and the pressure surge would have been avoided.

On the other hand if the closing time is too slow, then any pipeline or cargo hose failure, or a possible overloading, may result in unnecessary damage from cargo spillage.

The closure time should, therefore, be as quick as possible, but not so quick that large pressure surge occurs. The correct closing time depends on the length of the pipeline and the liquid flow. Instructions regarding this are given at certain shore installations. The danger is greatest when cargo is pumped over a long distance at too great a speed.

It is generally accepted that a 5 - 10 sec. closing time is adequate for most loading terminals.

From the gas theory we know that every pressure increase in a vapour/liquid mixture (at saturated conditions) results in condensation and every pressure decrease results in boiling. This is highly applicable to pressure variations in pipeline systems. We must, therefore, ensure that any pressure variations occur gradually, for example pressure increase when starting cargo pumps.

If, during valve throttling, we get cavitation noises in the pipeline due to boiling at the valve, and condensation immediately thereafter, it is worth taking the pressure drop over two valves in series.

The danger of pressure surge which can occur due to rapid closing/ opening of valves must be stressed to persons involved in the cargo operation.

Sea Water and Water Spray System

 

Generally

In the reliquefication plant on board seawater is used for the following purposes:

· Cooling of cargo- or R22 condenser
· Oil cooler for cargo- and R22 compressor
· Glycol cooler
· Heating of cargo during discharge
· Water spray system.

The sea-water pumps are placed in the engine room for supply to the cargo plant through a large bore pipeline. Overboard discharge with a non-return valve fitted between light and loaded waterline is normally arranged port and starboard of the cargo compressor room. The spindle connection for operation of the valves is fitted on the main deck.

Glycol System

For cooling and heating of the compressors there is a system arranged for circulation of glycol/water controlled by an automatic temperature regulator. Heating is done with an electrical heat exchanger placed in the electrical motor room, or by use of a steam heat exchanger. A sea water heat exchanger is used for cooling of the water/glycol.

This system can also be used for vaporising of the cargo in a vaporiser or in the cargo condenser.

Cargo Heating With Sea Water

When discharging cargo, which is to be heated up for delivery, nearly all the sea water is passed through the cargo heat exchanger. Therefore only a small part of the cargo reliquefication plant can be utilised.

The heat exchanger has manual valves for regulating the sea water supply to the required flow. The sea-water flow is dependent on the required temperature or pressure. However, it must be noted that if the flow of water is too fast, it will cause erosion (corrosion), and if it is too slow over a long period, marine growth can block the system. Sea water should not be left standing in the heat exchanger for a long period as this can result in corrosion damage. Rinsing with fresh water and sealing off the heat exchanger after use should be considered.

During heating of the cargo its temperature is controlled by regulating the cargo by-pass valve of the heat exchanger. It is obvious that, if propane is heated from _400C, there is a danger that the heat exchanger may be damaged by ice if the sea water circulation should fail. An alarm is therefore arranged, which will be activated on low pressure and low temperature at the sea water outlet.

Water Spray Systems

To keep equipment, cargo area and parts of the ship cooled during a fire the following areas are equipped with a spray system:

· Uninsulated tank domes
· Cargo loading/discharging area
· Uninsulated cargo tanks above deck
· Cargo compressor - and el. motor room external bulkheads
· The whole of the poop front.

On ships carrying propylene oxide there is, in addition, water spray on all P.O. carrying pipelines.

The capacity requirement is 10 liter/m2 per mm. for horizontal surfaces and 4 liter/m2 per mm. for vertical surfaces. The water is spread as evenly as possible through nozzles. The piping systems are normally made of galvanized steel.

The water supply can be arranged from the supply to fire mains, cargo plant, other water pumps normally used for other services or a separate water spray pump. The spray system can be activated from several positions on board, normally at the poop front, cargo control room or bride, at port and starboard cargo discharge/ loading manifolds and at the forecastle.

On ships carrying propylene oxide there is, in addition, thermal release of the water spray. In a pneumatic pipeline loop there are melting fuses in different locations placed in the cargo area. In the event of a fire the fuses will melt if the temperature reaches abt 1000C. This will release the air pressure in the system and thereby activate the water spray (see Fig. 3).

Gas Boil-Off as Fuel

Using the boil-off from the cargo as fuel for the main- or auxiliary machinery is only of interest on LNG ships. It would be too expensive and complicated to reliquefy the LNG boil-off on board. A reliquefication plant for this has been designed but has not yet been installed. The feasability of installing such plants in the future will depend on the price relation between bunkers and LNG.

The boil-off can be used as fuel in boilers on turbine ships, in "dual fuel" diesel engines and in gas turbines, with the first alternative being the best.

The quantity of boil-off is about 0,25% per day, or lower. For a 125000m3 LNG turbine tanker this will supply abt 75% of the fuel requirement to maintain full speed, whilst for a 87000m3 LNG tanker it will represent abt 60%.

The gas is sucked from the cargo tanks by compressors and led through a sea water heat exchanger. From the heat exchanger the gas is piped through the supply line to the boiler room/engine room. A quick closing valve is placed in the gas supply line in front of the poop deck. From there and down to the boilers or engines the gas supply line is installed in a mechanically exhaust-ventilated pipe or duct. In front of the utilizing unit (eg boiler/engine) there is a flow regulating valve and two automatic shut-off valves in series. The gas in the pipe between these two valves is vented to atmosphere through the vent mast.

In a boiler the burners are designed for both oil and gas in any mix. ratio, except that a minimum of 5% oil has to be used as a pilot flame to ensure reliable gas combustion. The classification societies have detailed requirements regarding the safety automation for shut-off systems activated by alarm conditions in the system. See Figure 4.

The gas supply line has to be purged with nitrogen when shutting down the machinery in port.
