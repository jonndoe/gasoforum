Introduction

At the end of this topic you will find several useful data sheets, tables and curves. These shall be used in future correspondence letters. Of gases that are suitable for transport by ship, we have only included thermodynamic curves/tables and data sheets for ammonia, butane and propane so as to save space. If you understand how to use these it will be easy to use similar tables and curves for other gases.

You will find a thermodynamic curve page for both the gases ammonia and n-butane (normal-butane), whilst for propane there are several curve pages and a table of thermodynamic properties.

In front of the curve pages you will find a description of the symbols used in the curves and tables, and in front of the data pages you will find a list of definitions and expressions used, as well as a code for toxicity.

 

Use of Data Sheets and Curves

 

Use of Curves

First we shall see how the thermodynamic curve pages can be used. Note that such curves apply only for boiling liquids and for saturated vapour pressure, unless otherwise indicated (therefore not for subcooled liquid or superheated vapour).

Example 1:

You have been informed that you are to load a cargo of ammonia. The cargo temperature from the shore installation has been given as _50C. What will the tank pressure be in the cargo tanks if this temperature is maintained?

Answer:

Go in at _50C on the temperature scale at the bottom of the curve page for ammonia (X-axis), and follow this temperature line vertically up to the curve labelled "p" (the curve for saturation pressure). From this point of intersection go horizontally out to the left to the pressure scale that is also labelled with a "p". Here we read off 3,5ATA (`ATM' is an old-fashioned term and stands for absolute atmospheric pressure - i.e. 3,5 times atmospheric pressure). The calculation is:

saturation pressure = 3,5 ATA
-atmospheric pressure = 1,0 ATA
tank pressure = 2,5 ATO (Atmospheric Overpressure)

_ 2,5 bar

Example 2:

A tank is loaded with ammonia. The tank pressure is read off the manometer as 2bar (~ 2ATO). What is the liquid temperature in the tank?

Answer:

First we calculate the saturation pressure of the ammonia:

tank pressure = 2ATO
+atmospheric pressure = lATA
saturation pressure = 3ATA

Thereafter we use the curve sheet for ammonia with a saturation pressure of 3ata on the pressure scale.

From the pressure scale we go horizontally out to the saturation pressure curve (p). From this point of intersection go vertically down to the temperature scale and read of f -90C. In other words the temperature of the ammonia cargo is -90C.

In the same way as in the preceeding examples where we found the saturation pressure using the liquid temperature, we can find the density (specific weight) for liquids and vapour from these curves using temperature. Curves for liquid densities are labelled Y', and curves for vapour densities are labelled with a Y". The scales that belong to these curves are labelled with the same symbol.

Let us now look at a few examples!

Example 3:

A tank contains n-butane at +400C; what is the density of the liquid?

Answer:

From the temperature scale go vertically up to the density curve for n-butane liquid (labelled Y'). From this curve go out to the scale for liquid density (also labelled Y'). Here we read off 0,555kg/dm3. So the density for n-butane liquid at +40°C is 0,555kg/dm3 (or 555kg/m3).

Example 4:

In an inter stage cooler (in the reliquefication system) we have both liquid butane and butane vapour (above the liquid surface) with a temperature of +400C. Assume the liquid and vapour are in balance (boiling liquid and saturated vapour). What is the vapour density in the inter stage cooler, and what is the pressure in the tank (manometer pressure)?

Answer:

The density of the vapour is found from the curve labelled (Y").

The density for saturated butane vapour at +400C is 9,65kg/m3.

The pressure in the inter stage cooler is found as before, from the curve labelled (p). The saturation pressure for butane liquid at +400C is 3,76ATA The manometer pressure is thus abt. 2,76AT0

Remember: (saturation pressure minus atmospheric pressure = over-pressure)

(Over-pressure = manometer pressure = tank pressure)

Example 5:

In a tank containing butane we have read a manometer pressure (tank pressure) of 1,8ATO. What is the density of the liquid butane?

Answer:

First we calculate the saturation pressure:

Over pressure (manometer pressure) = 1,8ATO
+ atmospheric pressure = 1,OATA
saturation pressure (absolute pressure) = 2,8ATA

Using the saturation pressure (2,8ATA) we find that the temperature of the liquid butane is +300C (the "p"-curve). With +300C on the temperature scale we go up to the density curve for liquid butane (the Y' curve) and find that the density of the liquid at this temperature is 0,5675kg/dm3.

Example 6:

We are going to purge a cargo tank with propane vapour before loading (i.e. we are going to replace the existing tank atmosphere with propane vapour. The existing atmosphere could for example be inert gas).

The ship takes on board 10m3 of liquid propane at _410C. How many m3 of propane vapour will this constitute at 1bar absolute pressure (atmospheric pressure), and with a tank temperature of +150C when the liquid has boiled off?

Answer:

For propane we have a complete table for thermodynamic properties. This table is used in preference to a curve as this will give us more accurate results than a curve would. In the table the density of liquid propane at _400C is found to be 0,57966kg/1 (= 579,66kg/m3). First we calculate the mass of the liquid propane:

Mass of liquid: Volume·density = mass

10m3·579,66kg/m3 = 5796,6kg

Using this mass (5796,6kg) and the density for super heated propane vapour (at +150C and 1bar) we can find the volume of the propane vapour. The density for propane vapour is found from the curve called "The density for superheated vapour - propane" by using +150C and 1bar, and is found to be 1,75kg/m3.

The calculation will be:

Volume (vapour) =

5796,6kg
1,75kg/m3

= 3312m3 (vapour)

By taking on board l0m3 of liquid propane at _410C, we have made 3312m3 propane vapour that will replace the existing tank atmosphere.

Look also at the figure "comparison of densities for a few uncondensed gases in relation to air". Here you can see that propane vapour is more than 1,5 times heavier than air, whilst inert gas has approximately the same density as air. To get the best possible "piston-effect" during a purge operation it is important to know this. In our example (No. 6) as you understand, it is important to release a possible inert gas out from the top of the tank(s). whilst propane vapour (which is heavier than inert gas) is supplied to the bottom of the tank(s). This applies only to the first exchange.

Definitions of Expressions used in Data Sheets

Toxicity: The ability of a substance to produce injury once it reaches a susceptible site on or within the body.

Danger: Possibility to be injured if one handles or uses the substance in the quantities, or in the way mentioned.

Acute: Refers to a single or sudden event or reaction, that is to say an acute affect or an acute illness.

Chronic: Refers to an event or reaction that re-occurs or is long-lasting, that is to say a chronic illness.

Local: Refers to a well-defined area of the body that becomes damaged or that reacts, and means that the effect takes place on the part of the body that comes in contact with the substance.

Systemic: The effect of a substance or its vapour on those parts of the body with which it is not in contact. This presupposes that absorption has taken place.

Absorption: A substance is said to have been absorbed only when it has entered the blood circulation, and in this way is spread to all parts of the body.

Toxication Level

0 - Nothing a) Not damaging in any circumstances.

b) Damaging only in special circumstances or in very large quantities.

1 - Weak Causes changes in the body. These changes disappear when the effect is removed.

2 - Moderate Can include both temporary and permanent changes that do not lead to death or serious injury.

3 - High Can result in death or serious injury even though the effect may be of low concentration or for only a short time.

U - Unknown No available information that we regard as being valid concerning the effect on people.

References:

N. Irving Sax: "Dangerous Properties of Industrial Materials".
Van Nostrand Reinhold Company.

Description.

At room temperature and atmospheric pressure ammonia is a colourless, alkali vapour with a sharp smell. Ammonia is a very stable substance. Fission has a tendency to occur at temperatures from 84°C to 930°C at atmospheric pressure. Ammonia dissolves in water, methanol and ethanol, whilst oil barely dissolves in ammonia. If there is water present, ammonia will bind with carbon dioxide and form ammonium carbonate (NH4)HCO3. (NH4)CO2NH2. Under special circumstances ammoniumkarbamat, NH4CO2NH2, and ammonium carbonate NH4HCO3, may also form if carbon dioxide is present.

The named substances consist of a white, crystalline powder which can clog up pipes and regulating vents and may result in damage to compressors and pumps. Inert gas that contains CO2 must, therefore, not be used in connection with ammonia.

Ammonia mixed with hydrogen or free nitrogen atoms forms an explosive mixture when there is air present.

Hazard analysis:

Toxication level:

Acute local: Irritation 3, swallowing 3, inhalation 3
Acute systematic: U
Chronic local: Irritation 1
Chronic systematic: U
TLV (Threshold LimitValue): 50 ppm

Inhalation of high concentrations of ammonia will result in severe coughing due to irritation in the air passages. If you do not quickly get away from such an atmosphere, it may result in serious lung irritation, lung damage and bronchial catarrh, which may result in death. Lower concentrations can result in eye irritation, bronchial catarrh and bronchitis.

Swallowing of liquid ammonia results in serious corrosive damage in the mouth, throat and stomach.

If one is subject to higher concentrations of ammonia vapour, it may lead to a passing blindness as well as serious eye damage. If the ammonia comes in direct contact with the eyes, it will result in serious corrosive damage to the eyes. Water-free ammonia that comes in direct contact with the skin will result in "burn sores".

Fire hazard: Moderate, in connection with heat or naked flames.

Expl. hazard: Moderate when subjected to open flames. In connection with silver or mercury an explosive mixture forms.

Accident hazard: Medium danger. Can blow up gas containers with explosive force. Releases poisonous gases if heated.

Fire extinction: Carbon dioxide, powder extinguishers or water.

Anyone working with ammonia must use rubber gloves, protective glasses and a rubber or plastic apron. A gas mask of approved type, and water (shower or eye fountain) must be available in case of accident.

Even though ammonia does not represent a great fire hazard, mixtures of air and 15 - 28 volume% ammonia ignite from sparks, or if the mixture is subjected to temperatures above 650°C. Open flames and sparks must, therefore, not occur in areas where ammonia is to be found. The explosive danger for ammonia/air mixtures is less than for hydrocarbon/air mixtures, because ammonia/air mixtures have higher ignition temperatures and principally because ammonia mixtures have a much higher ignition energy (680mJ for ammonia compared to 0,2 - 0,5mJ for propane).

Because ammonia is easily dissolved in water, a non-return valve, or similar, should be used on ventilation hoses so that water is not sucked into them. Such injected water can mean that extremely corrosive atmospheres can form in a tank. In addition there is also the possibility that injected water may result in a violent reaction with ammonia left behind in the tank, which can result in a vacuum and subsequent collapse of the tank.

First-aid.

Send for a doctor as soon as possible if someone has been involved in an ammonia accident. Whilst waiting for the doctor, first-aid must be given. The following notes are those usually practised in industry, but a doctor should be contacted before the first-aid procedure is decided on. The following can be used as a guideline:

The injured person should be taken to a heated room (ab. 22_) that is ammonia-free. The patient must rest for at least 24 hours. The patient must lie on a slope with the head and shoulders elevated, and must be kept warm, if necessary with blankets or similar.

If suffocating.

If breathing is weak, give oxygen or a mixture of oxygen and max. 5% carbon dioxide. (This mixture can normally be obtained from local fire and police stations and hospitals, along with required apparatus.) If breathing has stopped, give artificial respiration. This must only be carried out by trained personnel. Artificial respiration given by an untrained person is a great danger with ammonia injuries, and should be avoided as far as possible. The use of a pulmotor is not recommended as the powerful movements will irritate and damage the lungs. Oxygen equipment operated by trained personnel is recommended.

The eyes.

Keep the eyelids apart and pour clean water over the eyeball and the eyelids (or use an eye-fountain). Thoroughly wash in this way for about 15 minutes. A doctor, preferably an eye specialist, should be called.

The skin.

Quick help is very important. Remove ammonia-damp clothes from the body immediately. Rinse the affected skin areas continuously with clean water for at least 15 minutes. Burn sores caused by ammonia must not be covered by bandages or similar, but must be left open so that air reaches them.

Nose and throat.

Rinse the nose and throat with clean water continuously for 15 minutes. If the patient can swallow, get him to drink large quantities of 0.5% citric acid solution or lemonade. Call the doctor as soon as possible.

First aid equipment.

It is recommended that a bottle of 0.5% citric acid solution is accessible at all places where ammonia is handled. Likewise a clean, heated room should be accessible outside the area where ammonia is to be found.

Searching for leaks

Leakages can be found by passing an open bottle or a spray-bottle filled with concentrated hydrochloric acid over areas where a leakage is thought to be. Thick, white ammoniuin chloride vapour will form at the leak.

Wet, red litmus or phenolphtalin paper changes colour in an ammonia atmosphere, and can be of help when searching for ammonia leakages. Large leaks that cannot be repaired soon enough, should be sprayed continuously with water. The water will absorb the ammonia and stop the atmosphere being contaminated.

Construction materials.

Most normal metal construction materials are not affected by water-free ammonia.

On the other hand, if the ammonia is mixed with small quantities of water or water vapour, the ammonia both in vapour and liquid form will violently attack copper, silver, zinc, tin and all alloys that contain these substances. Iron and steel are not particularly affected by "dry" or "damp" ammonia, and are usually used as materials in pipes and fittings.

The pipe system should be completely welded, except where short connections are necessary, e.g. between cylinders and manifolds or between pipelines. In these areas, flexible steel strengthened neoprene pipes are recommended.

Ammonia can combine with mercury to form an explosive mixture. Instruments that contain mercury should, therefore, not be used in connection with ammonia.

Ammonia as a cooling medium

Water-free ammonia is one of the oldest commercial cooling mediums known. It is also used in both absorption systems and compressor installations, and can, with advantages, be used where its toxicity can be accepted.

The heat of vaporisation is quite high, and ammonia is specially suitable for use in piston compressors. At low vaporisation heat it can, however, be used in systems where a centrifugal compressor is being used as a booster-compressor.

A relatively high superheating can occur, even at moderate pressures. This superheating can be reduced by cooling after each step in a multi-stepped process.

For lubricating oil in an ammonia compressor a low refined cheap oil can be used. This is because ammonia barely dissolves in mineral oil, and it will, therefore, not affect the viscosity of the oil to any particular degree.

Propane as a Cooling Medium

Elastomers are difficult to use as a packing material in connection with propane as most of these will swell or disintegrate. Metal jointing is mainly used.

Compared with propane, ethane is a volatile constituent and will, therefore, boil quicker than propane in the tanks. The vapour that is sucked into the compressor has consequently a higher ethane content than the liquid cargo. Cargo Handling Equipment

 

Main Systems in the Cargo Handling Installation

We constructively try to reduce the possibility of gas leaks from tanks and pipe systems. This is expressed as follows:

· Thorough choice of materials and control.

· Comprehensive design/testing of tank construction and main components.

· Appropriate arrangements

The Pipeline System

The cargo is transported through the ship's pipeline system and loaded on board as well as discharged ashore. The pipeline system also gives the possibility for ventilation, inerting, heating and cooling of the cargo.

The system is completely closed so as not to allow air in or gas to leak out. The pipeline system can be opened manually for connection with the ventilation mast (the gas mast).

The pipelines are equipped with safety valves that will open to the gas mast if the pressure should increase to the limit set on the valves.

Pipes and flanges are made of nickel/steel alloy so that they tolerate the low temperatures of the cargo that the ship is constructed to carry.

The pipelines are also fitted with expansion bellows to compensate for expansion/contraction and movements of the cargo tanks.

The pipeline systems for loading can be split into the following groups:

· Pipelines for cargo in a liquid phase
· Pipelines for cargo in a vapour phase
· Pipelines for the cooling system (sea water)
· Pipelines for inert gas and ventilation
· Pipelines for various auxiliary systems

The pipeline system is often split into two or more separate systems. This means that the ship can carry different cargoes at the same time. The systems can, if so wished, be separated from each other with removable spool-pieces. Such a separation is necessary when carrying two different cargoes where even small mixtures, due to leaks through closed valves, are undesirable.

On deck there are normally one or two main pipelines for liquid and vapour that are connected to separate crossovers. The liquid main pipeline is connected to the pumps and the loading line in each tank, whilst the vapour line is connected with the cooling system for the cargo.

On a fully pressurised ship the pipeline system is normally very simple and is made up of a combined loading and discharging line as well as a vapour-return line. Fully pressurised ships do not have a cooling system or pumps for discharging the tanks. To discharge, over-pressure is built up in the tanks, thereby pressing the liquid out. The tanks are pressurised by using a compressor on board, or from shore.

The pipeline system on a medium-pressure ship is more complicated. In addition to a cooling system, these ships are also equipped with pumps and a system for heating cargo at sea or during discharging.

Atmospheric-pressure ships have pipeline systems that are similar to those on medium-pressure ships, but usually not as extensive. The ships are equipped with a cooling system.

The Cooling System

As long as two connected mediums have a temperature difference, heat will pass from the warmer to the colder medium.

When the colder medium is a liquid at boiling point, all the additional heat will be used to vaporize (boil off) the liquid. The liquid temperature will, therefore, remain constant as long as the vapour is conducted away.

At atmospheric pressure different liquids boil at different temperatures. For example, water boils at +100°C, ethylene boils at -104°C, and pure propane boils at _42°C. The boiling point increases as the pressure over the liquid increases.

The great temperature difference between cargo tanks and their surroundings means that there is a flow of heat from the surroundings to the cargo. As a result of this the cargo will start to boil. When the vapour does not escape from the tanks, the pressure over the liquid increases as well as the temperature of the liquid. Some of the additional heat will be consumed by warming up the liquid.

If the cargo tanks were constructed to tolerate a high pressure, this process would continue until the temperature of the cargo was the same as its surroundings. For example with an air and propane temperature of +30°C, the pressure in the tanks would rise to about 11 bar.

However, cargo tanks are not constructed to tolerate such pressures. To satisfy the requirement of low tank pressure and temperature, it is necessary to remove the boiled-off vapour, re-condense it and feed it back into the tanks.

This is possible by using the reliquefication plant.

The task of the reliquefication plant will be, therefore, to transfer the gained heat from the cargo to sea water to hinder loss of cargo.

The reliquefication system composition depends upon the ship's size as well as the cargo the ship is built to carry. There are three main cooling systems that are used:

1. Direct condensing with sea water.

2. Indirect cooling.

3. Cascade system.

Direct Condensing with Sea Water

A cooling system with one or more stages with direct sea water condensing is used on LPG ships of up to about 15000m3. Cargoes such as propane, butane and ammonia are cooled in this way. The principal mode of operation in a two-stage system is shown in figure 19. Cold vapour is sucked from the top of the cargo tank through a liquid separator and into the low pressure side of the cargo compressor.

Due to the slow speed of the vapour in the liquid separator trap any entrained liquid droplets are removed. The vapour is compressed and then pressed into the interstage cooler through a pipe that enters at the bottom of the cooler. The cooler is about half full of liquid. The vapour bubbles through the liquid and is cooled before it is sucked into the high pressure side of the compressor.

After the second stage compression the vapour is then pressed into the sea-water-cooled condenser where the vapour is condensed on the outside of the pipes by heat exchange with sea water on the inside of the pipes.

From the sea-water-cooled condenser, the condensed cargo drains down into the cargo liquid receiver. This acts as an effective liquid trap. The liquid level in the receiver controls the regulating valve for the liquid return to the cargo tank.

From the liquid receiver the warm liquid is fed in two runs to the interstage cooler. A part is fed direct into the cooler through an expansion valve controlled by the liquid level in the interstage cooler. The rest is fed via a coil to the same cooler through an expansion valve back to to cargo tanks. Due to the heat exchange with cold liquid on the outside of the coil, the condensation in the coils is sub cooled.

The reason for direct injection of the condensate to the inter-stage cooler is to compensate for the liquid that evaporates in the cooler. This is due to the heat exchange with the liquid in the coil and the warm vapour from the low pressure side of the compressor.

During the starting stage the pressure in the sea-water-cooled condenser can increase due to the collection of inert gas. This results in a reduction in the capacity of the reliquefication plant. To avoid this, it is necessary to blow off the inert gas to the gasmast or back into the tank until the condensing pressure has again stabilised itself. A continual venting ("bleeding") aids the capacity of the condenser.

Indirect Cooling

With indirect cooling we have a system where the cargo is not used as a cooling medium. It is a closed system that usually uses R22 as a cooling medium for the reliquefication plant.

There are three different systems for indirect cooling found on gas ships:

· Condensing of the boiled-off vapour in a heat exchanger outside the cargo tanks.
· Cooling of the liquid cargo in a heat exchanger outside the cargo tanks. (fig. 20).
· Cooling with coils fixed outside on the shell of the cargo tank.

Cascade System

If the vaporisation temperature (the cargo temperature) and its corresponding pressure is low, the necessary pressure increase from the evaporator to the condenser will be quite high. This applies to a cargo such as ethylene.

This will result in a reduced sucking effect in the compressors, and the mechanical load on the compressors may also be too high. This can be avoided by compressing the gas in several steps.

Another problem that may occur when the cargo is at too low a pressure is that the vapour temperature in the high pressure stage may become too high. This is due to the heat that is generated during compression.

Instead of compressing the vapour in several stages, we can make use of two independent cooling systems (cascade system) that work at different temperature levels. Both systems can be either one or two-stage, but the principle is as shown in fig. 21.

In the low temperature systems the cargo is often used as a cooling medium, and it will condense in a heat exchanger that is cooled by an evaporating secondary medium.

By doing it this way, the high pressure that otherwise would have been needed for condensing by using sea water in a direct cooling process, has been avoided. As a secondary medium halocarbons are usually recommended (a chemical bond between carbon, hydrogen fluor and chlorine). R22 is often used. Such a cooling system is normally called a "cascade system", just as heat transport can be thought of as a cascade (primary medium - secondary medium - sea water).

Cargo Side

The cargo side of the cascade system is constructed as a two stage direct cooling system with intermediate cooling. The low pressure side of the compressor sucks vapour from the liquid separator, and delivers it to the intermediate cooler as indicated in fig. 21. During cooling the intermediate cooler is filled with liquid, and the vapour is sucked off as saturated vapour to the high pressure side of the compressor. The pressurised vapour is then delivered to the cargo condenser where the vapour is condensed against boiling R22.

The condensate is then fed through a cooling coil in the intermediate cooler where the condensation is sub-cooled to a temperature of about 5°C above the temperature on the liquid side.

After cooling, the condensate is returned to the tanks through a regulating valve controlled by the level of the liquid in the cargo receiver. The liquid level in the intermediate cooler is maintained by letting condensate in through a separate regulating valve. It might be required to run steps 1 and 2 in parallel for propane and other cargoes with a higher temperature. The pressurised vapour from the compressor then goes direct to the cargo condenser without intermediate cooling.

R22 Side

The secondary system is a one-stage system with a screw compressor. The system is made up of two circuits, one with circulating R22 liquid and vapour between the cargo condenser and the R22 separator. Liquid R22 is pumped with the help of two parallel circulation pumps from the liquid separator drop-line to the cargo condenser. In this 10 to 20% of liquid R22 is vaporised so that a R22 vapour/liquid mixture streams back into the liquid separator.

In the other circuit the R22 compressor sucks vapour from the liquid separator, and the pressurised vapour is fed to a condenser cooled with sea water. The pressurised vapour passes through an oil separator where most of the oil in the pressurised vapour is separated out.

After the receiver, condensed R22 is led through an expansion valve back to the liquid separator.

Auxiliary Systems

Inert Gas System

If a flammable vapour is present there are three requirements that must be satisfied, at the same time, to allow the vapour to explode.

The three requirements for combustion to take place are presence of flammable vapour, presence of oxygen (air) and ignition. The proportions of flammable vapour to oxygen (air) must be within the flammable limits (between the Upper Explosive Limit (UEL) and the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL)). To reduce the risk of an explosion these conditions must be avoided.

Inert gas is used as a common description for gases that cannot form explosive mixtures. The inert gas must, therefore, not contain oxygen, or so little that the explosion limit is not reached.

Inert gas is used to free tanks and pipelines of cargo vapour. First the cargo vapour in the tank is displaced with inert gas, then it is ventilated with air. In some circumstances inert gas is also used directly in connection with changing of cargo.

The procedure is: Cargo vapour Inert gas Air

By doing it this way the afore-mentioned conditions are not met, and there is no danger of an explosion.

Inert gas is produced on board in a so-called inert gas generator (see fig. 22) by removing oxygen from air using combustion. The combustion occurs with the least possible surplus air. The exhaust gas will then contain only a small amount of oxygen, normally abt. 0,5 vol%. The main constituents of the exhaust gas will be 84% nitrogen, 15% carbon dioxide and traces of soot and carbon monoxide.

To avoid or reduce condensation in the inert gas pipelines and the cargo tanks, the gas must be dried. This is done in a washing tower (scrubber) where the inert gas from the combustion chamber flows upwards in direct contact with a downwards flow of seawater. This results in two desired reactions. First, the gas temperature is virtually reduced to the temperature of the sea water, thereby significantly reducing the humidity by condensation. Besides which, the sulphur dioxide in the inert gas combines with the water to form sulphuric acid, which is washed out by the sea water together with the condensed water. After being through the washing tower the inert gas will have a water content that is equivalent to 100% relative humidity at that temperature.

The dew point is, thereafter, reduced to about +5°C in the inert gas cooler. At this temperature the water content in inert gas is about 5g/kg (or about 7g pr.m3 inert gas).

As a combustion process is used for producing the inert gas, it must not be used in connection with ammonia due to its carbon dioxide content. This is because the ammonia will react with the carbon dioxide and form ammonia salts as ammoniumcarbonate and ammoniumcarbamate. These salts can block pipes, valves etc.

Stationary Gas Detector

As mentioned, most cargoes that are transported by gas ships are both flammable and explosive. This means that those mixtures with cargo vapour and air or oxygen could burn or explode if the conditions are right.

Classification Societies require that the area where the chance of a leak is greatest must be carefully planned by way of the positioning and choice of equipment. This area must be designated as the gas dangerous area, and includes:

· Cargo tanks
· Area surrounding tanks
· The deck
· The compressor room for cargo
· Various rooms adjoining the cargo area.

On board every gas ship there is a permanently-installed stationary gas detector (see fig. 23). This sucks gas samples from different places in the gas dangerous area, as well as the engine room and the electric motor room. An automatic control system assures that the gas samples are taken in correct sequence, and that there is no more than 30 minutes between tests at each point. An alarm sounds when the gas concentration has reached 30% of the lower explosion limit.

Discharging Pumps

Later in this course discharging pumps will be properly covered. Here we are only going to consider the general principles.

Discharging pumps on board liquefied gas ships are of the centrifugal type. For such pumps a variation in back pressure will give a corresponding variation in delivery capacity. Increased back pressure will give a reduced capacity. Further, their effect requirement will decrease with increased back pressure. This is because the effect requirement is more affected by the capacity than the pressure increase.

Deepwell Pumps

Discharging pumps on LPG-ships are normally electrically powered deepwell pumps where the pump unit is placed in the bottom of the tank and the motor outside. To remove as much of the cargo as possible, the pump unit is placed in a well (sump). When using the pump correctly there should, therefore, be little cargo left at the end of the discharging.

Booster Pumps

Booster pumps are used together with dicharging pumps when these alone cannot manage to give sufficient pressure. This would be either due to the high resistance of the shore pipeline system, or when discharging to shore tanks with cargo at a higher temperature (and hence higher pressure).

Booster pumps are also used together with heat exchangers when the delivery temperature to shore is to be higher than that of the cargo on board. Booster pumps are one-stage centrifugal pumps that are either placed on open deck or in the compressor room.

Cargo Heating System

Occasionally there may be times when the cargo has to be heated during transit or whilst discharging, e.g. because the storage tanks at the terminal do not tolerate such low temperatures due to the quality of steel used in the tanks and pipelines.

For heating during transit or discharging there is a sea-water heat exchanger installed on open deck. The cargo can be pumped through it and back into the tank or to shore.

In the sea-water heat exchanger the cargo is circulated in the pipes whilst the sea water flows freely in the heat exchanger. To ensure that the sea water does not reach such a low temperature that it freezes when it gives off its heat to the cargo in the pipes, there is a temperature sensor installed that gives an alarm at low sea-water temperature. The sensors automatically stop the booster pumps if there is a danger of the sea water freezing, thereby stopping the flow of cargo through the pipes. For heat exchanger capacity calculations see figure 24.

Some LPG ships also have a steam heat exchanger installed. In this the cargo is also circulated through pipes whilst steam is passed round them to heat up the cargo in the pipes.

Due to the low heat capacity of steam, such a system cannot heat up the cargo quick enough for discharging. However, a steam heat exchanger can be used to feed warm vapour to the tanks during unloading to prevent a vacuum building up in the tanks. It can also supply warm vapour to the cargo tanks in connection with gas freeing (boiling of f excess cargo).

Cargo Blower

A cargo blower delivers the warm vapour from the steam heat exchanger, or it can be used to deliver air to the tanks for ventilation during gas freeing.

Nitrogen System

Some ships have a nitrogen system installed. This applies to ships that can carry Propylene Oxide (PO) where the nitrogen is used to maintain the overpressure in the cargo tank atmosphere.

Ships with their own nitrogen plant that can produce nitrogen from the air, deliver either directly to the tanks or to a nitrogen storage tank.

Some ships have a set of several nitrogen cylinders, each having a volume of 50 litres under a pressure of 200 bars. Each cylinder has, therefore, a capacity of 10m3 of nitrogen at 1bar.

Purge Tanks

Some ships have extra deck storage vessels (purge tanks) installed for storing, for example ammonia and propane. The idea is to save time whilst changing cargo from one type to another. The cargo tanks can be gasfreed and then purged by using the gas in the purge tanks. These are often smaller tanks placed on deck. These tanks must be able to withstand high pressures (l8bar for ammonia and 15bar for propane) as they store the gases at ambient temperature. It is, therefore, important that IMO's requirement for the maximum filling levels is adhered to.

Pneumatic System

The pneumatic system is used for the following:

1. Emergency Shut Down-system (ESD)

a. Control system

b. Service system

c. Individual opening and closing

d. Emergency closing

2. Cooling system.

Description

Compressed air is delivered from the engine room to deck via a dryer so that the air has a dew point at about _40°C. The air dryer is normally a "heatless dryer", that is, it is based on the principle of chemical absorption of humidity.

On deck the air is fed through service air pipes to actuate the valve actuators, the Emergency Shut-Down system, and the sea-water spray. Service air is also used for the controlling of valves in the reliquefication plant.

Emergency Shut Down System

Classification Societies require that vapour and liquid connections on the tanks and to shore should be able to be closed remotely. Such need can arise from pipeline or hose breakages, fire etc. Remote closing is obtained by using pneumatic actuators fitted on the valves.

The control system should be under pressure to enable valves to be in open position, and when the pressure is released all the valves are closed at the same time (Emergency Shut Down). The control line is so arranged that the pressure can be released at strategic places on board, such as in the engine room, at the poopfront, on the bridge, in the compressor room, at loading discharging areas and from the forecastle. The control lines are fitted with meltfuses so that if the temperature rises over 100°C, for instance during a fire, the pressure is released.

Further, there are two pressostats installed that are activated by the control air. These stop all the cooling compressors as well as the deepwell and booster pumps if the control air pressure disappears, e.g. during emergency closure of the ESD-valves.

The service air system delivers air to all the actuators. The air supply is controlled by a three-way valve. This valve opens when the control air system is pressurized, and releases the air pressure in the service line when the control air disappears.

During normal use of the system it should be possible to open and close each individual valve manually. This is possible by installing a small three-way valve on each actuator.

At emergency shut-down all the open valves are closed when the pressure in the control lines is released. This can be done manually by opening one of the ESD release valves. In case of fire, the control air pressure will be released automatically when one of the melt-fuses melts at about 100°C. In both instances a valve will release the service air in the actuators into the atmosphere, whereas all the open ESD valves will close. At the same time the discharging pumps and the cargo compressors will shut-down.

Spray System

All surfaces that can come in contact with flammable mixtures must be kept at a lower temperature than the self-ignition temperature of the gas. All gas ships have to have a sea water spray system that covers:

· Tank domes
· Loading/discharging manifolds
· Poop front, deck buildings and control rooms that border with gas dangerous areas.

The spray system can be released from several places in the cargo area as well as outside gas dangerous areas. Likewise there are melting-plugs placed at all domes and at shore connections. These will melt in case of fire and will so release the spray system automatically.

Instrumentation

The instrumentation on board is arranged for a safe monitoring of all systems. In the gas dangerous area pneumatic or intrinsically safe equipment is used.

The instrumentation is quite extensive and, therefore, the larger gas ships normally have a separate control room for the cargo handling installation. The control room is situated in connection with the deck house and is not considered to be a gas dangerous area. From the control room the most important valves in the cargo system can be controlled. It also contains instruments for remote readings of pump pressure, tank pressure, cargo temperature, levels etc. The room has a mechanical over-pressure ventilation and, as in the electric motor room, there is a ventilated air lock between the open deck and the control room. The air intakes are placed so that there is the least possible risk that cargo vapour, vapour/air mixtures or sparks are drawn into the system.

The level indicators measure the liquid level in the tanks. Each tank has an indicator, and if one knows the cargo level, the ship's trim, the density of the cargo and the temperature of the tank, the amount of cargo on board can be calculated. The most common level indicators on gas ships are the so-called float type (see figures 25 and 26).

The indicator works by having a float hanging on a wire and floating on the liquid surface in the tank. The movements of the float are transferred to an indicator that can be read through an inspection hole on the top. For the indicator to show the true level it must be adjusted correctly. The top and the bottom readings on the level indicators are controlled against the stamped values on the top of the indicator house.

Further adjustments must be made for the density of the liquid, because the floats will float higher in a liquid with a high density than one with a low density

The measuring principle is that the resistance of a platinum thread submerged in the liquid is measured. Electrical Equipment in Gas Dangerous Areas

An effective way to ignite an explosive vapour/air mixture is by using an electrical discharge. For such a mixture to ignite by a discharge, the energy that is released must be greater than the minimum ignition energy for that particular cargo and mixture.

Certification of Equipment, Types and Principles

Requirements for electrical installations and equipment for gas ships are set by Administrations, Classification Societies, The International Technical Electrical Commission and IMO.

Areas and rooms are classified as "gas-safe" or "gas-dangerous" depending upon the possibility of vapour from the cargo being present. When an area is designated "gas-dangerous", all electrical equipment used in this area has to be built according to agreed rules and has to be certified safe for use in such areas, and for the particular gas. This applies also to transportable equipment. Certified equipment can be divided into "Intrinsically safe equipment" and "Explosion proof equipment".

Intrinsically Safe Equipment

Intrinsically safe equipment has an electric current that under normal use, or during failures, is unable to produce sparks that could ignite explosive mixtures.

The use of intrinsically safe equipment is limited to instrumentation and control and alarm systems. It cannot be used for high current equipment.

There is nothing called an intrinsically safe motor, even though the expression is sometimes used incorrectly in reference to a flame proof, explosion safe or increased safe motor etc.

Unluckily the abbreviation for both "increased safe" and "intrinsically safe" is "I.R".

The intrinsically safe installation (the relay) has a power source of 220V. The relay supplies a current with low voltage (8 - 12V) and a small current (abt. 5 mA)

The power source for an "intrinsically safe" installation is placed in a safe area and is isolated by the use of Zener barriers. This comprises Zener diodes, resistors and fuses. This barrier must never be changed or missed out as the low currents in intrinsically safe equipment are very sensitive and should only be inspected by skilled personnel.

Explosion Proof Equipment

Explosion proof equipment is recognised by the enclosure being strongly built so that an explosion in the equipment does not burst the cover.

This method is used for motors, coupling boxes, switches and similar equipment.

It is important that this type of equipment is maintained and installed so that it retains its original safety design.

General Precautions

Alarms and circuit breakers that are functioning must never be disconnected as this can put the whole ship in danger. Defective circuits can be by-passed, but this must be done with the knowledge of the Duty Officer and must be written in the log. Defective circuits must be repaired as soon as possible.

Certified safe equipment must be maintained by qualified personnel, and, in case of doubt, experts should be sought. Wires should be checked for wear, condensation, poor insulation, poor connections etc.

Additional electrical equipment must not be installed without it being approved by the relevant Authorities. This applies to both certified and uncertified equipment.

Portable electrical equipment, batteries or extension leads must not be used in cargo tanks, pumprooms, compressor rooms or in similar rooms except:

· rooms that are free of flammable gas at that time.
· adjoining rooms that are also free of flammable gases or have been made safe by filling with inert gas or by topping up with water.
· all connections with other rooms that are not gas free are closed and remain so.
· when the electrical equipment and extension leads are intrinsically safe.
· when the equipment is built in and is explosion proof. All flexible cables must be approved and attached to the equipment in the approved way.

Equipment that is only used on the tank deck can be explosion proof.

Air-powered lamps of approved type can be used in atmospheres containing gas. They must either be earthed or the hose must have such a low resistance that static spreading is impossible. Only certified gasproof safety torches can be used.

Smaller battery operated items such as wrist watches and hearing aids are insignificant lighting sources if they are used correctly. However, radios, pocket calculators, tape recorders and other un-approved equipment must never be used on the tank deck or in other places where gas is found.

In port the ship may lie in a gas dangerous area. The safety regulations in force regarding the use of electrical equipment in such areas must be respected on board.

The deck house where the reliquefication plant is arranged is in two sections, one gas dangerous room and another not dangerous (figure 27).

All the equipment that is connected directly with the cargo is placed in the gas dangerous room. That includes compressors, condensors etc. In the other room, the electric room, all the electric motors for the reliquefication plant, starters etc. are found. This equipment is not explosion safe or intrinsically safe.

Connecting passages between the compressor room and the electric motor room are gas tight. To enter the electric motor room from the open deck you have to pass a so called "air lock". This is a small and well ventilated room with a separate door to the deck and to the electric motor room. The distance between these two doors must be at least 2 meters. If both doors are open at the same time, the electric current to the electric motor room is cut, and an alarm sounds.

The electric motor room has a mechanical over-pressure ventilation, and the compressor room an under-pressure ventilation. That is, there is always over-pressure in the electric motor room in relation to the compressor room. The capacity of the fans are set so that the air in the compressor and electric motor room is changed 30 times an hour. A large gas leak would then be required before the lower explosion limit is reached. The fans are normally placed on open deck and, therefore, have explosion proof motors.

Answers from Page 70

1. All pipes in the cargo system are equipped with safety valves that open to the gas mast if the pressure in the pipelines increases above the set value of the safety valve.

2. In an indirect reliquefication plant a cooling medium in a closed system is used to cool the cargo (often R22). The cargo is cooled either by a coil (with vaporising R22) on the outside of the cargo tanks, or by circulating the cargo through a heat exchanger, or by condensing the vapour from the heat exchanger outside the cargo tanks.

In a plant for direct condensing the cargo itself is used as the cooling medium.

3. Inert gas is used as a common designation for gases that cannot form explosive mixtures together with other flammable gases.

4. The discharging capacity is about 225ton/h.

5. When the emergency shut-down system is activated, all the ESD valves on board the ship close as well as stopping all the reliquefication compressors and discharging pumps.

6. Temperature, pressure and level indicators.

7. Intrinsically safe equipment uses electricity with such a low voltage (8 - 12v) and low current (abt. 5mA) that, during normal operation or failures, sparks that can ignite an explosive gas mixture cannot occur.

8. Mobile electrical equipment can be used in the compressor room when:

a) The room is declared gas free, and adjoining rooms are free of flammable gases or are filled with neutral gas. Adjoining rooms (tanks) that are not gas free must be closed.
