Cargo Pumps - Liquefied Gas

 

Cargo Pump Design

In principle the various cargo pump designs (classifications) can be divided in two main groups and each split into several smaller groups dependent on their way of construction and characteristics. Fig. 1 shows a simple diagram of different pumps. The pump design is divided into two main groups due to the difference in the working-principle, one being the kinetic-pumps and the other the displacement-pumps. The cargo pumps on board a gas ship are of the centrifugal type (kinetic pumps). Common classification of the Centrifugal pumps are:

A. According to direction of flow

- Radial flow pumps

- Diagonal flow pumps

- Axial flow pumps

B. According to number of stages

- One stage pumps, ie. that only one impeller gives the pumps total lifting height. A one stage pump can have one or two suctions, depending on liquid flow on the suction side.

- Multi stage pumps, i.e. several impellers give the total lifting height.

C. According to required lifting height

- Low pressure pumps Head < 20 mLC
- Medium pressure pumps 20 > Head < 60 mLC
- High pressure pumps Head > 60 mLC

The given values for pump total head is only to be regarded as a guide.


Fig. 1

D. According to direction of the shaft

- Vertical pumps

- Horizontal pumps

The pump technology is large and comprehensive. But we shall mainly consider the part that is necessary and useful for our purpose and will enable us to use the cargo pump the right way, and in addition to calculate capacities using pump performance curves.

As a rule any variation in the pump back pressure will give a corresponding variation in the capacity. Increased back pressure will give decreased capacity. This relation can be drawn on a pressure/capacity diagram, normally called "the pump performance curve".

Further, the power consumption will decrease with increased back pressure due to the fact that the power consumption is more influenced by the capacity than pressure increase. The pump lifting height is equal to the difference between the delivery-and suction pressure. In practice this can be taken as the difference between the manometer reading on the pump and the tank pressure. The manometer readings are in bar, but most pump manufacturers give the pressure increase in metre liquid column (mLC), which is independent of the specific weight of the liquid. Later in this course we will study the relation between these two specifications for pressure as well as how we can calculate the pump delivery pressure.

The following points should be noted when operating cargo pumps.

· To avoid pressure surge, the gas cargo pump should be started with the discharge valve nearly closed. (The discharge value should only be opened e.g. 1 - 3 turns).

· The maximum allowable back pressure for the pump can be found from the pump performance curve.

· As the gas cargo is always at boiling point, there will be problems with cavitation at the pump suction side towards the end of the discharge (the pump is "losing the cargo"). The cavitation can be postponed by shutting the discharge valve, thereby increasing the back pressure.

· It is not allowed to run the pump dry (not pumping cargo) as the pumps are lubricated by the cargo. When cavitation occurs, there will be a drastic fall in power consumtion as the pump is running nearly without load. An electromagnetic circuit breaker will then cut the control current to the pump, and this will stop.

Deepwell Pumps

Cargo discharge pumps for LPG are normally of the deepwell type (Fig. 2), electrically powered, with the pump unit situated in the tank and the motor on the outside. To enable as much cargo as possible to be discharged the pump suction is placed in a well (sump). If the pump is operated correctly there will be very little cargo left on completion of discharge.

Booster Pumps

Booster pumps are used in series with the cargo pumps when these cannot deliver pressure high enough to overcome the back pressure created in the shore plant piping system, or when discharging to shore tanks with high cargo temperature (hence higher pressure).

Booster pumps are also used together with the cargo heater when the cargo discharge temperature is required to be higher than the cargo temperature on board. The booster pumps are of the one stage centrifugal type, either placed on the open deck or in the compressor room.

Centrifugal Pumps - Mode of Operation

The impeller in the centrifugal pump sets the liquid in rotation, hurls it outwards by centrifugal force against the pump casing, and creates a higher pressure on the discharge side. The energy added to the liquid is in the form of kinetic energy. If the discharge pipe had been carried up vertically above the deck, and had been open to the atmosphere, the liquid level in this would have been higher that the liquid level on the pump suction side. The difference in liquid level height would then have been the pump's net lifting height. The energy needed to lift the liquid to this height is the kinetic energy supplied by the pump. It can easily be seen that the work done by the pump is to transfer this kinetic energy to potential energy.

The pump lifting height is independent of the liquid density. However, the differential pressure will vary in proportion to the density. This is a criterion that is often misunderstood.

The centrifugal pump is mostly used to pump up to a fixed level, e.g. a tank or a reservoir etc., so that it is the total lifting height that is decisive, and this is constant, whether the pump is discharging propane or mercury. However, the pump power consumption is widely different for those two liquids, as the power consumption is proportional to the liquid density. This rule will apply to the centrifugal pump as long as the impeller revolution is constant..

Before the pump is delivered from the producer, a performance curve will be worked out based on "shop" tests and delivered together with the pump.

As the lifting height of the centrifugal pump (Delivery Head) is independent of the liquid density, it is natural to indicate the lifting height as a function of the pumping (delivery) capacity.

Some centrifugal pumps have a maximum delivery head at 20% - 30% of the maximum delivery capacity. This can be a disadvantage. The enclosed pump performance curves do indicate a small tendency to this effect. What can happen is that a pump with constant delivery head, in other words constant resistance, can give two different delivery capacities.

At the end it is worth mentioning that one of the disadvantages with centrifugal pumps is that the efficiency is high, but only inside a small delivery range. It should also be noted that some LPG pumps cover only a small range of densities.

Control Systems for Sentrifugal Pumps

The delivery range of centrifugal pumps can be varied either by altering the pump revolutions or by throttling the delivery valve (if desired also by running a bypass). As most centrifugal pumps are connected directly to the electrical motor with constant r.p.m. , the first method is less used on a gas ship. The most commonly used method of regulating the discharge is to use the centrifugal pump characteristic which enables us to throttle the discharge valve without damaging the pump..

Viscosity

Liquids with a viscosity of 5 centistoke or less have little influence on the pump performance-curve, but with values over this the pump effectiveness will decrease together with the capacity and the delivery head. As the cargoes on gas ships have a very low viscosity, we do not need to consider this parameter.

Condition for Pump Suction

The operation condition for the centrifugal pump on the suction side is decisive for the working of the pump. As the liquid is accelerated from the centre of the impeller and out towards the rim, it will create a temporary reduction in pressure. If the liquid is already at its boiling point, this reduction in pressure will result in vaporization of the liquid, and thereby form vapour bubbles. It has been discovered through many experiments that a normal centrifugal pump can operate satisfactorily with about 2% vapour in the liquid. On increasing the formation of bubbles above this, the consequence will be that the vapour bubbles collapse as the pressure is increased. This will create shock and erosion (cavitation) in the pump, consequently material damage. A variable discharging quantity can also be the result of vapour in the pump. To safeguard against vapour in the pump, we can make sure that the liquid on the suction side has the necessary over-pressure to avoid vaporization. The suction pressure can be improved by fitting the pump with an inducer. The inducer is a flow governour wheel fitted in front of the (first) impeller.

Cavitation

Cavitation is a well known phenomenon for engineers. Despite all the enormous effort put into this field, the knowledge is still incomplete. Cavitation in a centrifugal pump can arise when a liquid, either by temperature increase or by pressure reduction attains the liquid vapour pressure. Vapour bubbles or cavities will be formed in the liquid during pumping. If the conditions are altered, the vapour may be condensed and the bubbles collapse. This process will normally disturb the regularity of the liquid flow.

Cavitation may result in:

· damage to the impeller and housing (normally called erosion or pittings).
· noise, covering a wide spectrum.
· vibrations in the liquid flow and pump/motor.
· loss of efficiency.

All this acts in a destructive way, is unwanted and has either to be eliminated or controlled.

From what has been previously mentioned, it is clear that the liquid static pressure at the pump must not be reduced to the vapour pressure, if we are to avoid cavitation.

Cargo Pump Calculations

All modern gas ships are equipped with performance curves for the cargo pumps, and it is important that we can use such curves correctly. Figure 3 shows a diagram where a performance curve for a deepwell pump and the characteristics for the piping system are plotted. The characteristics for the piping are made up of the static and dynamic back pressure. The static back pressure is mainly the difference in liquid level in the ship's tanks and the shore tanks, and the difference between the tank pressure on board and ashore. The dynamic back pressure is the friction created in valves, bends and piping, etc.

What we call `the pump discharging pressure' is really the pressure increase in the pump, but as the tank pressure is near to zero, the pressure increase in the pump is therefore equal to the discharge pressure.

We can change the dynamic back pressure by e.g. throttling the pump discharge valve. Let us consider that we shall reduce the discharge rate from 940m3/hour to 600m3/hour by throttling the manifold valve. What will the manometer reading on the tank dome show?

Pumps Run in Parallel

If the ship is to deliver a larger quantity of cargo per time interval than the capacity of one pump, we normally have to use two or more cargo pumps in parallel. Fig. 3 shows how we can calculate the delivery quantity (and discharge head) when two pumps are run in parallel.

Pump curve "A" gives the delivery quantity (690m3/hour) and the delivery head (100 mLC) when only one pump is in use.

Curve "TV' shows the combined pump curve for two (equal) pumps run in parallel. As the piping characteristics remain the same, the new delivery rate is 970m3/hour and the delivery head is 134mLC. The performance curve for two pumps run in parallel is the added x-axis values (delivery capacity) for each pump at different delivery heads. The delivery quantity for one pump at 20mLC is e.g. 965m3/hour, hence the delivery quantity for two (equal) pumps run in parallel, will be 1930m3/hour at 20mLC. If two pumps are run in parallel, the best (cheapest) way is to have one common throttling valve on the discharge line. Any possible imbalance between the two pumps can thereby be counteracted.

Pumps Run in Series

If the ship shall deliver cargo with higher delivery head than possible with one pump, we normally have to run two or more pumps in series. On board a gas ship this is usually done by using one booster pump in series with one or more deepwell pumps.

To find the combined capacity curve for one deepwell pump that is run in series with one booster pump, we have to add together both the pumps y-axis values (delivery head) for the different capacities. We will not show an example of this here, as the problem is easy to understand.

Cargo Operations

 

Preparation for Loading

Prior to arrival of a ship in port the cargo operation should been planned, so that the necessary loading time can be cut as short as possible. This implies that the ship is made ready for the forthcoming loading, and that information regarding port requirements is acquired..

The information of most interest is:

· Type of cargo
· Pressure and cargo temperature as well as other cargo properties.
· Flange dimensions on shore connection.
· If the terminal can receive vapour return and receiving capacity.
· Available loading rate.

With received information about cargo and the terminal, the ship is made ready for loading:

· Carrying out pre-cargo calculation.
· Make the tanks and piping system ready.
· Positioning of valves, necessary blind flanges and spool pieces.
· Fitting reducer to the manifold connection, if necessary.
· Testing the ship's cargo emergency shut down system.
· Prepare and make ready the ship's fire extinguishing equipment.

On arrival at port the following is carried out:

· The earthing cable from shore is connected on board. This is often not used as the earthing cable is connected from the loading arm. In certain ports it is required to turn off the induced currency hull antifouling system.
· Loading arm and vapour return line are connected, pressurized to check for leakages. The loading arm will often be cooled down during this operation.
· The cargo tanks are checked together with surveyor for pressure, temperature and ullage.
· Normally sampling of cargo residues in the cargo tanks is carried out, and in certain cases also sampling of the tank atmosphere.

This procedure is based on the fact that we had the same type of cargo on board on the previous voyage, i.e. no change of cargo grade.

Loading of Inorganic Gases

Prior to loading of ammonia (NH3) the cargo tanks and piping system have to be filled with ammonia vapour, if possible, e.g. if the ship carried ammonia on the last voyage. If there is no ammonia vapour in the cargo tanks prior to loading, the tanks shall have a nitrogen (air) atmosphere. It is not quite right to load NH3 against air. However, if NHg is to be loaded in tanks filled with air, then the air atmosphere has to be purged with NH3 vapour. The routine with NH3 purging of cargo tanks filled with air is somewhat doubtful as it is possible there is a flammable mixture present in the cargo tank. NH3 must never be loaded in tanks filled with inert gas produced from oil-based inert gas producers.

Loading of Organic Gases

Most of the organic gases that are loaded on gas ships are LPG gases. By LPG we mean first and foremost propane, butane, propylene, iso-butane and butylene. Prior to loading these gases, the piping system and tanks must be filled with inert gas, nitrogen or cargo vapour of same grade. The mentioned gases will normally be considered to be equal in this respect, but the charterer's requirement has to be taken into consideration. Inert gas that is produced on board contains mainly abt. 85% N2 (nitrogen) and 15% CO2 (carbon dioxide).

The tanks will normally be filled with air before the inert gas is filled. As the air is displaced by the inert gas, the oxygen content in the tank will be reduced from 21% (clean air) down to the required O2 concentration. If the shore terminal does not have a particular requirement for maximum oxygen content, then a reduction of the oxygen content down to 5% oxygen will be adequate. In this case we have only considered the danger of explosion, but for certain unsaturated cargoes it is required to have a much lower oxygen concentration due to chemical reactions (Normally below 0,5% prior to loading).

Loading With Vapour Return to Store

Loading with vapour return to shore is used when the shore plant has facilities for receiving the vapour, and that there is a possibility for connecting up the vapour line (Fig. 5).

In addition to vapour return, the reliquefication plant should be used so that the loading rate can be maximized. The loading rate has to be reduced if the tank pressure becomes too high.

A sudden closure of the vapour return on the shore side can happen. If the ship's safety valves are opened, the emergency shut down system should be activated.

If one cargo tank should be over filled, then some cargo can be transferred to other tanks. Newer ships have installed automatic closing valves that will close at high liquid level in the tank. Traces of other, more volatile gases in the tank atmosphere (nitrogen etc.) may give a higher pressure than expected.

Loading Without Vapour Return

Firstly, information regarding the delivery temperature and pressure of the cargo to be loaded from the shore plant has to be received. If the ship is to load cargo from shore tanks, with a higher temperature than the safety valve set point, then the reliquefication plant has to be run (Fig. 6) to cool down the incoming cargo to a temperature lower than the safety valve set point. The capacity of the reliquefication plant will, therefore, determine the loading rate. Secondly, it has to be established where the cargo is to be discharged and at what delivery temperature. If the tank pressure increases to an unacceptable value then the loading rate has to be reduced. The loading rate can be increased by altering the safety valves to a higher set point. On the other hand it is necessary to ensure that the pressure is sufficiently below the safety valve set point to avoid unintended release of vapour to the atmosphere.

Loading from Barges (fully pressurized tanks)

With a barge we mean a floating installation from which the ship is receiving cargo and where the installation has no cargo pumps or compressors with which to transfer the cargo. The cargo in such tanks will normally be at ambient temperature and the pressure will be accordingly high.

It is the pressure in the barge's tanks that forces the cargo over into the ship's tanks.

This way of cargo loading may take place for such cargoes as LPG and ammonia, and mostly for semi-pressurized ships.

The-whole reliquefication plant should be run at maximum output, and it is this plant that will determine the loading rate.

Termination of Loading Operation

When required tank filling is reached, the filling (loading) valve to the tank is closed. The pressure, temperature and ullage are read off together with a representative from the terminal, so that the quantity of the cargo loaded can be calculated.

The remaining liquid in the loading arm/loading hose can be removed in several ways depending on available methods.

a) The Terminal can blow the loading arm/hose with nitrogen, inert gas or hot vapour to the cargo tank before disconnecting.

b) One of the ship's cargo compressors can be started, building up a vapour pressure in the cargo piping up to the manifold. The manifold valve is then opened and the -cargo residues are blown back to the shore tank and/or the ship's tank.

c) The shore plant may have the possibility to blow the cargo residue to a flare for burning.

d) Another possibility is to connect up to the ship's gasventilation mast, or use a hose overboard, and let the cargo residue boil off. However, this is a questionable method with respect to the governing regulations.

If the cargo is cold it will be sensible to have running water in the area beneath the manifold during disconnection of the loading arm/hose, thereby quickly removing possible spillage and protecting against cracking of the deck plating.

Only after the shore connection is removed, should the earthing cable be disconnected.

Depending on the cargo condition after the loading and the condition required on delivery, it can be decided what cargo operation will be necessary during the voyage.

Cargo Cool-Down

If the cargo is to be delivered at a temperature that is equal to or lower than the temperature on completion of loading, then the cargo has to be cooled on the voyage. Information is available on board the ship stating how long it takes to cool down the cargo from one temperature to another. From this the ship can decide in advance what is the maximum allowable cargo temperature on departure at the loading port so that the necessary cool down can be performed on the voyage.

Cargo Heating

When the ship has loaded cargo cooler than what is to be delivered to the receiver, then the cargo has to be heated during the voyage or during discharging.

The filling limit shall always be 98% when the cargo temperature corresponds to the safey valve set point..

The lower the set point is, the more cargo can be carried. But a cold cargo needs longer time to be heated. Therefore, it will be an economical evaluation of what shall be done in each case.

E.g.

a) To load fully refrigerated cargo against a high set point, which will give less loaded quantity, but will allow heating during the voyage, hence shorter discharge time, or

b) to load fully refrigerated cargo against a low set point, thereby longer discharge time.

Heating during the voyage will be carried out by pumping the cargo with a deepwell pump from the tank through the cargo heater on deck and returning it to the tank (fig. 7).

Preparation for Cargo Discharge

Prior to arrival at the discharging port the ship should be as far as possible ready to commence discharging. One should, therefore, try to obtain information such as:

1) Delivery temperature

2) Back pressure at requested delivery rate

3) Vapour return

4) Cargo arm/hose sizes etc.

Based on this information, the discharging operation should be planned.

· Equipment to be used should be made ready.
· All valves to be used set in correct position.
· Emergency shut down system tested.
· Fire extinguishing made ready.
· Immediately after completion of mooring, the earthing cable to shore should be connected.
· In cooperation with representative from the Terminal, the liquid level, cargo temperature, pressure and the ship's trim are read and noted.

NOTE! Follow carefully the sampling of cargo and vapour.

· New cargo calculation is carried out.
· The cargo arm/hoses are connected and blown free for air. This can be done from shore and out through the "blow-off" valve at the manifold to the ship's gas mast, or from the ship by use of nitrogen/inert gas. Thereafter the vapour is led to the shore plant "blow-off" system.

Discharging to Pressurized Storage Tanks

With a high back pressure it will be necessary to run the booster pump(s) in series with the deepwell pump(s) (Fig. 9). If the ship's cargo is colder than what the shore installation can receive, then the cargo has to be heated in the sea-water cargo heater.

Control With Cargo `Heel' (Coolant) on Ballast Voyage

If the ship has no pressurized storage tank on the deck, and the ballast voyage will be of more than 4 - 5 days' duration, then it might be advisable to collect all cargo residues in one tank, and keep this tank cooled. Thereby the use of compressors will be kept to a minimum and give an economical saving.

Cooling Down of Cargo Tanks by Use of Cargo Heel

The last 2 days of the ballast voyage can be used to cool down the remainder of the cargo tanks, provided that these contain the same gas in the vapour atmosphere.

The actual cooling is done by using the condensate from the reliquefication plant, that has been used to keep the cargo residue cooled, to the tank that is to be cooled down through the spray lines if fitted. This condensate will evaporate quickly, and in doing so draw the heat from the tank shell and atmosphere, resulting in cooling down.

As the evaporation continues the tank pressure will increase, and one reliquefication unit has to be connected up to this tank for further cooling.

 

Change of Cargo

In certain cases the existing cargo vapour in the tanks will be purged with the vapour of the next cargo, and the cargo residue mixed with the next cargo. This may only be done in such cases where the cargoes are compatible and allowed by the charterers. However, often the following procedures have to be carried out:

· Removal of cargo residues.
· Heating tanks.
· Inerting of tanks with inertgas or nitrogen.
· Inerting of pipe-line system and reliquefication plant with inertgas or nitrogen.
· Purging of tanks, pipelines and reliquefication plant with vapour from the next cargo.

Cargo Tank Inspection

Maintenance and inspection of cargo tanks may include the following operations:

· Removal of cargo residues.
· Heating tanks.
· Inerting of tanks with inert gas of nitrogen.
· Inerting of pipe-line system with inert gas or nitrogen.
· Ventilation of tanks with air/possible tank washing with water.
· Ventilation of the pipe-line system with air/possible washing with water.
· Ventilation of the Void Space with air.

Puddle Heating

Cargo residues are normally removed by help of the cargo compressor. This will suck from the tanks and return the heated vapour to the tank bottom. The heat is given to the vapour by compressing to a higher pressure (higher pressure = higher temperature) in the compressor. As the hours go by the tank pressure will increase as more and more of the liquid in the tank bottom will boil off. It is an advantage to keep the tank pressure as high as possible, but not all the way up to the safety valve opening pressure. Excess pressure is therefore released through the gas mast, ashore or through the reliquefication plant to the pressurized deck storage tank.

This procedure is time consuming - 48 hours is not uncommon - all depending on the quantity of cargo residue and the boiling point of the liquid.

As the tank pressure increases, any liquid in the pump might well be pressed up the liquid riser and emptied that way. This will give less liquid to evaporate. This method cannot be used on atmospheric pressure ships. On ships with a reliable temperature sensor in the pump well, a sharp rise in temperature will indicate that all the liquid has evaporated.

The heating of the cargo tanks goes more quickly as all the liquid has evaporated. How much the tank atmosphere is to be heated up will depend on the dewpoint of the inert gas that we are to use.

If the tanks are to be purged with nitrogen, then it will not be necessary to heat as much, as the dewpoint of the nitrogen normally is very low.

Generally speaking it can be an advantage to heat the tanks up to +50C, if the time allows, especially if the tanks are to be vented with air after gasfreeing.

When the tanks are to be inerted with nitrogen it will be less expensive to use inert gas first, if available, and to take the final touch with nitrogen. Such a method can only be used if allowed by the charterer, as he might be afraid that any soot can contaminate the tank/cargo. Some ships have one or two blowers that can be used to heat up the tanks. The blowers will draw the vapour from the tanks, press it through a steam heater and thereafter return the vapour to the tanks. This method will save the cargo compressors as it may be harmful for them to be exposed to such high temperatures. This is due to the extremely high temperatures we will get in the compressors as the vapour temperature in the tanks increases.

Inerting and Gasfreeing

Inerting and gasfreeing normally take place at sea, either before drydocking or due to change of cargo. As only a few ships have enough nitrogen on board to inert all the cargo tanks, the inerting is carried out by inert gas produced in an inert gas producer. This consists of a fan, combustion chamber and a washing tower.

The oil is burnt in the inert gas generator with as low surplus air as possible, avoiding formation of soot. About 0,5% O2 is possible. The gas is wet on leaving the washing tower, (100% relative humidity), and must therefore be dried in a dryer. This is either done by a cooler or an absorption dryer. After drying, the inert gas is led through a flexible hose to the x-overs.

Previous experience will then normally help you to decide how to proceed with the inerting. Shall we begin with one tank on its own or several tanks together? This may be different from ship to ship. In general it can be said that it is an advantage to start with parallel purging of the tanks for the first filling, thereby achieving the best piston effect. Thereafter purge the tanks in series (from tank to tank), thereby maximising the use of the inert gas. If we are to enter the inert gas at the top or the bottom of the tank depends on the density difference of the gases. If the gas in the tank is lighter than inert gas, then the inert gas should be let in at the bottom of the tank, thus pressing the cargo vapour out through the vapour line at the tank top, either to the gas mast, x-over to shore or via the reliquefication plant to the storage tank on deck.

We can measure the inert gas concentration in the tank after a while. If everything has gone as planned, we should have a sharp division between the inert and the cargo vapour. This is what we call "piston effect". If the inert gas is allowed into the tank too fast, we will easily get a mixture of inert gas and cargo vapour. This is unfortunate as the operation will then be unnecessarily longer.

After a while when the tank is measured with HC/O2 gas measuring instruments and the reading is below LEL (Lower Explosive Limit), we can regard the inerting as completed. We can then start ventilating the tank with air, but only after we have measured the air dewpoint and found this to be lower than the temperature in the tank. For the ventilation we can use a fan, the cargo compressor or the inert gas fan (without the burner). Care has to be taken during the gasfreeing to avoid the explosion range. This can be ensured by measuring the atmosphere with the transportable HC-instrument and the O2-instrument.

In the case of ammonia we cannot use inert gas for gasfreeing as the ammonia will react with the CO2 in the inert gas and form ammonium carbonate, which is a white powder. Often air is used directly against the ammonia vapour for gasfreeing, but it is not really advisable. To avoid the formation of explosive mixtures of ammonia/air, nitrogen should be used. Nitrogen does not react with ammonia.

Procedure for Tank Cleaning

After the tank has been vented, the tank is entered to investigate if any work is necessary inside the tank. Often a thin dust layer on the tank bottom has to be cleaned up. If the tank shell is wet, it has to be dried, if possible. When tank cleaning after a propylene-oxide cargo (PO cargo), the tanks and pipe lines have to be washed with fresh water. Thereafter they shall be dried well by use of hot air. It is very important to remove all water from bends and traps in the pipeline system.

Mixing of Gases by Diffusion

Two gases will always mix together, even if they have different densities (sp. gravity). The "piston" effect we always aim to achieve when inerting or purging tanks, is only effective over a short period, and in the layer between the two gases there will be a diffusion taking place. If we shut off the tank where there is a defined layer between the gases, then the atmosphere will be a homogeneous mixture of the gases after a while. That is, the gases are completely mixed.

Solubility of Gases in Liquids

If LPG is loaded in a tank containing inert gas, then some of this gas will dissolve in the liquid. This will later be noticed when running the reliquefication plant. The inert gas in the reliquefication plant will be far above its critical pressure and temperature, and will not be able to condense in an ordinary ship's reliquefication-plant. This will lead to an undesired high pressure build up in the cargo condenser, as the inert gas will collect in this. The inert gas has, therefore, to be vented to the atmosphere.

When carrying propylene oxide (boiling point at atmospheric pressure = 33,90C) there has to be an automatic nitrogen "topping up" system that can maintain an over-pressure of min. 0,07bar N2 (padding) above the cargo. This is to ensure that no air can enter the cargo tank as the cargo vapour pressure at normal transport temperature (abt 200C) will be only about 0,6bar abs. (i.e. below the atmospheric pressure). It will, however, turn out that an over-pressure above 0,07bar applied to the tank one day, will have been reduced on the following day. This is not due to leakage (as could be believed), but due to the fact that some of the N2 has dissolved in the PG cargo (liquidL (Compare CO2 in lemonade).

Cargo Segregation

Most gas ships are constructed to carry two different cargoes simultaneously. To separate the cargo piping system, removable pipe lengths (spool pieces) are used. Thereby the systems are separated physically (positive segregation), so that there are no posibilities for mixing and hence contamination of the cargoes.

The following is an extract from IMO's Regulations regarding segregation of cargoes subjected to the most stringent requirements:

· Only spool pieces (not isolating valves) have to be used for cargo segregation if separate cargo piping systems are not available.
· Blind flange connection shall be fitted with a wire and seal through one of the bolts.
· A recognised and independent society (e.g. DnV) shall carry out an inspection on board on completion of segregation and sealing. Based on this inspection, a certificate is made out to verify that the piping system is segregated in accordance with approved drawings. The certificate is to be shown to a representative from the local authority (eg. USCG) prior to loading.
· Drawings showing segregation arrangements shall be available on board all gas ships.

Procedures for Transfer of Cargo

A cargo tank can be emptied if there is space available in the ship's other tanks. This transfer is done by use of the deepwell pump. The tank can thereafter be dried, heated, inerted and ventilated to allow access for inspection or repair work..

Cool Down of a Gas Free Cargo System, from Ambient Temperature, Procedure and Safety Routines

Cool down of a gas free cargo system will vary from ship to ship, according to how it is constructed and what cargo is to be loaded. The builder's recommendations have to be followed.

On LNG ships the tanks will often be inerted with own produced inert gas down to an acceptable O2 content. Thereafter the inert gas will be recirculated through a silica gel dryer until a dewpoint of _300C to _600C is reached.

This can also be carried out by using warm nitrogen until the same dewpoint is reached, but will be more expensive if the time loss is the same.

On reaching the dewpoint the cargo hose will be connected and warm methane taken on board. This gas is heated up to at least _100C before entering the top of the tanks. In this way the inert gas will be forced out of the tanks and piping.

The cool down of the cargo tanks can be started when the readings of the tank atmosphere show only methane vapour.

The tank purging will take about 20 hours, whilst the cool down period will be about 36 - 45 hours. The tanks should then be down to _1350C at the equatorial level, and the normal loading can then commence. The cargo liquid should not reach the equatorial tank level before the temperature is below _1450C.

With respect to ships loading ethylene (or ethane), the cargo tanks require pre-cooling (about the same as for LNG). The builders recommendations have to be followed.

The purging prior to loading ethylene is carried out with warm nitrogen. The inert gas produced by combustion is not clean enough, and the O2 content is too high. The dewpoint is very important. It should normally be down to _400C. Only then can ethylene purging commence. Normally the rate of cooldown of the tanks will be abt 100C per hour until _800C is reached. On reaching this temperature, ethylene loading through the loading line can start.

Nitrogen purging of cargo tanks is also required for butadiene (_50C) and vinyl chloride
(_140C), as the inert gas has too high a O2 content. By using nitrogen purging, the dewpoint requirement is normally not difficult to meet. The max. oxygen content for butadiene is often set at 0,2% and for vinyl chloride max. 0,1%.

Regarding the loading of ammonia (_330C) this is normally done after ammonia purging of air filled cargo tanks, as this is an allowed practice in several ports. The ammonia vapour is then entered at the tank top as it is lighter than air. When 98% of the tank atmosphere is replaced with ammonia, i.e. nearly all air or nitrogen is removed, the loading can commence. However, according to the regulations, (the Gas Code pt. 9.1.3.), the tanks should be purged with nitrogen prior to ammonia purging.

Ammonia must not be mixed with inert gas produced by combustion as the CO2 content is relatively high and will react chemically with the ammonia and form unwanted ammonium carbonates or ammonium carbamates. Even if ammonia can form an explosive blend with air, the danger of an explosion is less than for LPG/air blends. This is due to the fact that the ignition temperature for ammonia/air is much higher than that for LPG/air. The risk of ignition is therefore minimal, hence the ammonia vapour in the tanks are often ventilated by using air.

Hydrocarbon gases must not be mixed with air, as such gas mixtures have a large explosive range. The hydrocarbon gases have, therefore, to be purged by inert gas.

Loading of LPG can take place against inert gas or nitrogen. If the inert gas in the tanks has a dewpoint of +50C, water will be separated in the propane/inert layer. It is, therefore, important that the gas is led into the tank in such a way that the best possible "piston effect" is obtained without too much mixing of the cargo vapour and inert gas. Normally the propane vapour is loaded through the bottom pipe line and the inert gas is pressed out through the vapour line at the tank top. When the tank and pipe line system is filled with propane the "cool down" process can be started. The cargo loading can commence as soon as an acceptable average tank temperature is reached. Most tanks need not be pre-cooled prior to loading LPG (_550C). The cool down period differs from ship to ship. Therefore it is important to follow the ship's cargo operation manual. Too fast cooling can result in cracks in the cargo tank.
