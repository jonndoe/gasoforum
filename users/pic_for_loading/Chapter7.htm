Gas Measuring

In connection with inerting, purging and ventilating of cargo tanks, it is important to understand a flammability diagram and the theory behind gas measuring. Often it will be necessary to take gas measurements in other connections also.

The following gas measuring instruments are needed as a minimum on board a gas ship:

1) O2 - meter (oxygen measuring instrument)

2) HC - meter (hydro carbon measuring instrument)

3) Explosimeter (LEL - measurer)

4) Test tubes with chemical colour reaction

It is the Maritime Directorate (The Administration) that decides which kind of gas measuring instruments shall be approved for use on ships.

Before you take a gas measurement you must understand how the instruments work, and their directions for use.

Explosion Limits for Flammable Gases Mixed with Air

Combustion (and therefore the possibility of an explosion) can only take place if the conditions between the flammable gas and air are found to be between certain limiting values. These require that there must be the right proportional conditions between the number of molecules of flammable gas, and the number of oxygen molecules.

Fig. 1

The arrow in Fig. 1 shows the concentration of flammable gas in a mixture with air. A mixture with such a high concentration of gas that it cannot be ignited is called "Too Rich". "Too Lean" mixture is when the concentration of gas is so low that it cannot be ignited. The mixtures between these two areas are flammable and, if ignited, will either burn or explode.

The boundary between the too rich and explosive mixture is known as the "upper explosion limit", (IJEL).

The boundary between the too lean and the explosive mixture is known as the "lower explosive limit", (LEL).

There are tables giving the lower and upper explosion limits for mixtures of air with different flammable gases. Laboratories in several countries have decided these explosion limits, and the values given will depend upon the conditions in which the ignition is carried out.

Different gases have different flammable characteristics. Fig. 2 shows the LEL and UEL for various flammable gases mixed with air.

Explosion Limits for Different Oxygen Concentrations

It is a characteristic for the role oxygen plays in a combustion reaction, that the intensity of a fire or explosion increases drastically if the atmosphere has a higher oxygen content than air (Fig. 3). Note the extra danger factors that will occur if, in connection with use of welding equipment, an oxygen leak occurs, where for example work clothing will burn so intensely that it is virtually impossible to extinguish.

A further characteristic role oxygen plays, is a reduction in fires and explosion risks when the oxygen content in the atmosphere is significantly lower than in air.

If the oxygen content is reduced to less than 10,8 vol.% in an atmosphere containing hydrocarbon gas, the gas mixture cannot be ignited. However, there is an international agreement that the oxygen content in a cargo tank is never to increase above 5 vol.% O2 before loading a hydrocarbon gas. The upper explosion limit decreases significantly and the lower explosion limit increases somewhat when the oxygen content in a gas mixture is reduced..

Corresponding characteristic curves for the boundary between explosive and non-explosive gas mixtures can be found for other flammable gases than hydrocarbon gases. The values determined experimentally will be different if either air mixed with nitrogen, air mixed with carbon dioxide or air mixed with combustion gas is used. For many flammable gases there are no published data about these conditions..

Flammability Diagram

For flammable gases we can present the flammable area in a diagram (ref. Fig. 3). In the diagram we have chosen to put the air/oxygen content on the x-axis, and the hydro carbon gas content on the y-axis.

The illustration (Fig. 4) shows the flammability area for propane vapour.

Fig. 4

The left side of the diagram (y-axis) shows the hydro carbon gas content (HC), whilst the scale at the bottom of the diagram shows the oxygen content (O2) Note that 21% O2 corresponds to pure air (100% air). We normally assume that 10,8 volume% O2 is the minimum oxygen content required for ignition of the gas/air mixture. At lower oxygen content the gas/air mixture will not be flammable.

The LEL (Lower Explosion Limit) for propane gas is 2,1 vol%, whilst the UEL (Upper Explosion Limit) is 9,5 vol.%.

We measure the HC content in a tank using an HC-meter, for examp1e an "Interferometer". The oxygen content is measured using an O2- meter, for example a "Servomex". These two measurements together should really show us any mixture conditions between flammable gas (HC) and oxygen (O2), but due to several reasons a third type of gas measuring instrument, an "explosimeter" is used.

The explosimeter (e.g. Riken GP204) is used to measure flammable gas in air below the lower explosion limit, and is only suitable for measuring lean mixture of flammable gas in air. The instrument covers two important requirements. Firstly it can give an accurate measurement, even if there are small quantities of MC in the atmosphere that is being measured, something that is difficult to measure with an HG meter. We are also able to have a double control of our gas measurements when the tank atmosphere is below the LEL.

In addition to the above mentioned gas measuring instruments, the so called "test tube with chemical colour reaction" (e.g. Draeger Multigas Detector) is used.. These test tubes can also be used for small concentrations of MC gas and other gases. The tubes are easy to use and can, if desired, give us an accurate extra control if needed.

Let us now do a small thought-experiment.. We feed the tank a set amount of propane vapour at point "E", and move therefore straight through the flammability area up to point "G" (not allowed in practice for a cargo tank), where we measure 12% HG and 19,2% O2 The vapour at point "0" is too rich to burn, but if we had taken a gas test at point "F", it would have been flammable.

At point "G" we begin to feed inert gas to the tank. The inert gas generator produces inert gas with 1% O2 content, and we therefore move towards point "I". If we should begin ventilating the tank with air at point "H", we would again move towards point "E", and would soon find ourselves in the flammable area once again.

Flash Point

The flash point of a liquid is found by using an instrument in which the liquid is slowly heated whilst various attempts are made to ignite the vapour just above the surface of the liquid. The temperature at which the liquid finally ignites is called the flash point, and is usually given in degrees Celcius.

The value given for the flash point depends upon the apparatus and the speed of heating, and methods are usually referred to as "closed cup" and "open cup".

The flash point for most condensed gases carried on board gas tankers will be much lower than 00C (see Fig. 8).

Health Hazards

 

The Effect of Poisonous Gases on the Body

Many of the different liquid cargoes that are carried on board ships have certain health hazard properties. Data sheets show us to what degree the products are a hazard to our health.

The ability of a substance to do damage when it either comes into contact with the skin or is taken up into the organism is known as the poisonousness or toxicity of the substance. These poisonous substances can be found in several different forms, such as dust, vapour, smoke, liquid or gases.

The harmful effects of a substance have been known for a long time. As far back as 1531 a man called Aureolus Theophrastus von Hohenheim found that children that worked as chimney sweepers contracted cancer of the testicles far more frequently than others. It was due to the tar in the soot they were in contact with, and that they also used their work clothes during their leisure time.

Gradually we realised the risk run by being exposed to different substances, and scientific toxicology resulted. Toxicology is the science of poisons; that department of pathology or medicine which deals with the nature and effects of poisons.

Later a gradual classification of the harmful effects of the different substances was begun, and eventually rules and laws were laid down to protect against harmful substances. In Norway it is the Law of 1977 relating to worker protection and working environment with latest ammendments that is the statutory provision.

In toxicology chemical substances are divided according to their poisonous level:

1. Poisonous (e.g. Prussic acid)

2. Allergy forming (e.g. some cleaning agents)

3. Corrosive (e.g. Methyl bromide/chloride)

4. Irritable (e.g. ammonia, chlorine, hydrochloric acid)

5. Cancerous (e.g. VCN)

6. Hereditary detriment (e.g. dioxide)

7. Damage to the foetus (e.g. thalidomide)

There is an agreed working level (threshold limit) that gives the highest allowed concentration of a substance in the air at the place of work (air pollution). The concentration is given in ppm (parts per million) for e.g. the number of cm3 of gas per m3 of air. The hygienic working value of the substance is known as TLV (Threshold Limit Value).

TLV was decided on by the American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists, and the values are continually updated according to new information. Normally new information about the danger of a substance results in the TLV values being halved, according to previous values. This is done so to avoid long-term damage, which is of course not a guarantee that damage does not occur.

The intention to limit values is to prevent those working in polluted areas from being exposed to harmful amounts of poisonous or toxic substances.

The limit values are set with a view that one can work within the same environment for 8 hours per day for many years without being harmed. The TLV can be seen as an expression for the mutual toxicity of various substances. Let us point out that most of the liquids transported by a gas ship have a low degree of toxicity.

Previously there was a widespread understanding that we were able to smell if a dangerous gas was present. It is true that most gases have a smell, but in many cases, before we notice that smell, the limit value for our tolerance may have been passed long ago.

If there is no specially given value for the toxic danger for a substance, 500 ppm is often used as a limit value (0,5 0/00).

Poisonousness (toxicity) is a property linked directly to the product and is unchangeable. Deciders for the hazard factors are:

the substance
how it is used
in what quantities
how often it is handled

The environment has a direct influence on people's helth, and in this connection the most important factors are sufficient ventilation and whether safety equipment is in use.

The Body's Function

We shall look at circulation, breathing and the activity of the brain to understand better what happens if we are exposed to a gas leak.

Circulation

The main organ in the circulation (cardiovascular) system is the heart which works like a mechanical double pump. The blood, which takes up oxygen from the lungs, goes to the left part of the heart and is distributed around the body through the arteries to the cells. The cells take a little oxygen from the blood and release carbon dioxide in its place. The heart pumps blood around the whole body by pulling itself together and widening the artery walls, which we can register by taking the pulse, averaging 70 to 80 pulsations per minute in an adult, somewhat faster in children and slower in elderly persons. The blood circulates back through the veins on the right-hand side of the heart and then to the lungs, where carbon dioxide is freed and a new supply of oxygen is collected for the recirculatory cycle to begin again.

The human body is composed of billions of living cells, all of which need oxygen to survive but vary in their requirement. The brain cells need the most oxygen and, therefore, are the first to suffer if the supply of oxygen is restricted or cut off.

Fig. 9
Respiration

The main organ of the respiratory system is the lungs. The chest muscles and the rib cage expand and contract around the lungs in the same way as bellows, when we breath normally. Air is drawn into the lungs through the mouth and nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea and bronchi.

In the upper part of the larynx there is a valve which guards against the entry of liquids or solids into the wind-pipe. When unconcious, this valve can fail, and liquid substances can enter the bronchi and lungs, causing respiratory difficulties that can result in suffocation and death.

Fig. 10

A person normally breaths in and out 16 - 18 times per minute. Any event that can result in paralysis and stoppage of the respiration system (e.g. electrical shock) or that stops the oxygen supply to the lungs (e.g. drowning) can result in suffocation. As oxygen supply is essential to life, death will quickly be the result if it is stopped. Artificial respiration can restart the oxygen supply and save life (see the section on the method of artificial respiration).

The Brain

The brain has an extremely complicated construction that consists of many millons of cells.
The task of the brain is to receive `signals' from the special nerves that are connected to our senses (sight, smell, hearing etc) and so take the necessary decisions to carry out the actions by sending signals to the nerves that are connected to the different muscles in the body.

The brain acts as a storage space for memory. Simply speaking we can say that the brain consists of two main parts, the large brain with the higher brain centres and the smaller brain with the controlling and executive functions (lower centres).

The higher centres consist of millions of infinitely fine, small cells that control the senses. The thought capability, decision capacity, smell and hearing are found here. When a person is subject to reduced oxygen content in the blood, it is these cells that are damaged first.

The lower centres in the smaller brain consist of cells that are a little more robust than the cells in the higher centres. They control the body and muscle functions such as respiration, blood circulation and the body temperature. They, are to a great degree, self-regulating and control the body functions without receiving signals from the higher centres. For example the higher centres are resting when we sleep, whilst the lower centres keep the body function working.

An extended meaning of the word "sleep" is unconsciousness, which again is a condition of insensible senses which occur when there is a function failure in the higher brain centres, and we lose the ability to feel and understand impressions.

Unconciousness can be for longer or shorter periods, and can vary from a half-unconcious state (lethargy), which is difficult to get persons out of, to a total unconscious state (coma) which is impossible to wake a person from, even if we use all possible methods.

Unconsciousness can occur in connection with concussions, compression of internal bleeding in the brain, oxygen shortage, suffocation, drowning or gas or smoke effects. Unconsciousness can also occur due to fainting from fear, great injuries, bleeding, exhaustion, sun stroke, heart stroke and several other causes; all of which can be summed up in the expression `shock'.

Intake of Poisonous (Toxic) Substances

We can be harmed by poisonous substances by inhalation to the lungs, absorption through the skin or swallowing. The body will react to the effect of the harmful substance, and we can get the following reactions:

1. General symptons - local irritation (e.g. headache, nausea)

2. Accumulation in the organism (e.g. asbestos in the lungs)

3. Effect on special systems (e.g. lead in the bone marrow, or suffocation due to lack of oxygen)

4. Genetic damage (e.g. cancer of the liver from VCM)

In some data sheets we have available on board, the lethal dose (LD) is given. It is usually given as 50% of LD. We will also find information about RAT which stands for dose one is exposed to over time, so called long time effects. RAT is given in units of 20mg per kilo of body weight.

There is little difference in the practice of stating health hazards between the various data sheets. Those printed by ICS (International Chamber of Shipping) and found in the ICS Tanker Safety Guide for Gas Ships have clearly shown the hazards the products can represent. Other data sheets have numerical indentification for hazards, from 0 to 3, or from 0 to 4. You can find examples of these data sheets and classification of danger groups in Letter 3.

Some substances are dangerous because they can be absorbed by the body, others are not poisonous but can be irritative or corrosive. The expression toxic and the grading of the substances given in the data sheets are used more practically than literally. For example, note is only taken of inhaling or physical contact with the substance, and not the long time effect (chronic).

An environmental assessment for the work place is often based on personal experience; the level is based on earlier gained experience, and changes are often difficult to introduce in a work place. No one wishes to be called a quarrelsome person, and the environment often remains the same without any improvements in the conditions.

The blood is our most important distributer of absorbed toxic substances, and acts as an active depot. Solvents can, for example, damage the central nervous system permanently, and many people who today suffer from such long term damage are in such a state because changes were not made in time at the place of work.

As mentioned earlier, it is the higher brain centres that are affected first by oxygen scarcity. The same applies to a poisonous or drugging substance that has entered the blood.


Once a poisonous substance has entered the blood, it quickly reaches the brain cells. Poison in the blood depriving the cells of oxygen is also a contributory factor to unconsciousness, and in some cases death. Consciousness depends upon the higher brain centre. When this is not functioning any longer, the result will be unconsciousness.

As the effect of the poisonous gas (or scarcity of oxygen) increases, the lower centres that control the chest muscles are affected, and the victim stops breathing. Shortly afterwards the heart will stop and death will occur.

The effect caused by drugging or poisonous gas, will depend upon the gas concentration, and how long one is exposed to the effect.

Intake into the body:

When a poisonous substance is absorbed by (taken into) the body, there is normally another substance that is secreted. Host poisonous substances are broken down, mainly in the liver, kidneys, gall bladder and in the intestines. It is important for us to have a healthy liver and good kidneys, as it is here that most poisonous substances are broken down and later separated out.

The skin disposes of broken down waste by sweating, the kidneys via the urine channels and the lungs via the air channels.

Methods of Poisoning

We shall look closer at how poisoning can occur on board a gas ship when one is exposed to the affects of dangerous products.

Inhalation

After a gas release on board, there are several different ways the gas can affect us. We can be at risk of being suffocated due to the fact that the supplied gas will change the composition of the air. The more air that is expelled, the greater the danger of suffocation and scarcity of oxygen. If the oxygen concentration falls to 19,5% we are down to the lower acceptable level, and below 16% it will give serious health damage. At lower gas concentrations localised affects may damage the mucous membrane in the nose, throat and lungs, or the poisonous gas can enter the blood in the lungs.

A local effect on the mucous membrane will, as a rule, just be an irritation, but in rare cases it may be like mild burn sores. The effect is fortunately only mild, except for e.g. chlorine which will give milder burn sores, and will soon disappear.

On absorption into the blood via the lungs there will be a systematic effect because the blood will distribute the poisonous substance around the body. Some substances will give both an irritation and a systematic effect. Some substances that irritate may also give cramp in the bronchias when inhaling, and give breathing difficulties in the same way as with asthma.

Substances that are themselves not irritants, can still give allergic effects that for particularly sensitive people can feel like real asthma.

Irritable substances can also result in asthmatic breathing difficulties in the first instance, but allergy giving substances require several effects before this results in a full asthma attack.

It is, of course, true that different people react completely differently to various substances under the same conditions. It has been shown that when a group of people are exposed to the same poisonous substance at the same time, some may die of their injuries, some may become sick, whilst others are unaffected.

So, it is shown that the factors that have great importance are already existing conditions. One who already has liver damage from misuse of alcohol will, for instance, be in a higher risk group than others. Other factors of importance will be on how deep and rapid the breathing is, and if the body is in good physical condition. Someone who keeps his body in form, and who does not misuse medicines or other substances which may reduce the effect of the liver and kidneys, will be more immune to the effects of poisonous substances.

Penetration of the Skin

Some gases and chemicals have the ability to penetrate the skin and thereby rapidly enter the blood. This applies to only a few substances that we can come in contact with.

The factors that decide whether a substance will penetrate through the skin and so enter the blood are, for example, the chemical properties of the substance, the speed of the skin penetration, the poisonousness, influence time and the skin surface at the affected area.

Clothes that are damp from contact with the dangerous substance may act as a bandage, thereby increasing the length of skin contact. It is important that the contaminated clothes are removed immediately to reduce contact time. Local effects of the substance on the skin can also be expected, and for cold gases it may result in frost injuries.

When the substance has penetrated the skin, it will be absorbed by the blood which will carry it around to the organs of the body, so the effect will be in all body systems. It happens that a poisonous substance can be absorbed though a sore or by injection e.g. from high pressure equipment.

Swallowing

Some substances that connot easily penetrate the skin can be quickly absorbed by the mucous membrane in the mouth. On board a gas ship it is difficult to think that anyone will come into contact with a poisonous substance in such a way that it is swallowed, except in special circumstances, such as where high pressure may have forced the substance into the mouth, or with voluntary intake e.g. methanol.

Local Effects

Whilst on board a gas ship there are few instances where the crew are exposed so that a poisonous substance is inhaled (after large gas release), penetration of the skin (hole in a cargo hose or "blow out" of a gasket) or swallowig of poisonous substances. It is the local effects from "change" contact with the gas cargo that must be avoided. However, if we are exposed to the effects of gas, we must know how it affects us, and what can be done to reduce the harmful effects.

Skin Contact

In the case of a gas release on board, e.g. at a manifold or in a compressor, the cold gas will give the same reaction on the skin as strong heat would. Tissue will freeze depending upon how large the affected area is, the time of exposure and how quick the cooling was.

Everyone that is involved with cargo handling must carry the approved safety equipment. This will immediately reduce the extent of the injuries, but it is also important to remove clothing immediately after contamination. Strategically placed showers are found within the dangerous gas area, and it is important that persons contaminated by cargo liquid are immediately put under the water shower. This, together with the removing of clothing, will reduce the risk of frost damage of skin tissue.

Lighter skin contact with a gas will normally only give a mild and passing redness and smarting, but skin contact can also lead to serious injuries that can be compared to first and second degree burns, to completely destroyed skin tissue.

With frost damage the reaction of the skin during and after the exposure will show us the level of injury and can be categorised as burn damage. During heating, the skin will first go blue-red, then red and it smarts and swells up. If the attack is serious, the nervous system will initially stop the pain being felt, and pain-free frost damage is therefore the most dangerous.

Local effects of a greater extent can affect the body, not just the visual damage, but it may also disturb the body functions so that an injured person will become seriously ill (danger of death), even if there is no external poison effect, as for example penetration of the skin.

One of the most serious consequences of the body receiving frost damage by being exposed to cold gas, is that the fat and oil substances of the skin, the ones that are normally present to keep the skin soft and supple, are changed. The skin becomes dry and scaly, it cracks easily and thickens. Such lack of fat in the skin makes it vunerable to bacteria infections and for this reason, causes it to take a long time to heal.

Another consequence of frost damage is that it is easy to lose large amounts of liquid from sores which can be deadly, and there is also a high danger of infection. As mentioned earlier about risk groups and susceptibility to injury under similar conditions, healthy persons are able to stand local frost damage and poisoning better than depressed, sick or alcholised persons.

Eye Contact

The eyes are particularly vulnerable to any gas release, and it is, therefore, important that safety goggles are used in the dangerous gas area. However, if gas does get into the eyes, the most common harm will be irritation shown by redness, tears and a smarting feeling which makes it difficult not to rub the eyes.

More serious damage can result in the cornea being affected, either in the form of a dry itchy feeling with varying levels of pain, or in adverse cases where the gas burns many small holes in the membrane, which can lead to loss of sight. Sores that result from gases are particularly dangerous because they have a tendency to become worse due to incorrectly administrated first-aid treatment.

Treatment of Unconscious Persons

A gas ship is an independent unit where the possibilities of treating injuries to personnel depends upon the ship's medical equipment, but primarily on the crew's knowledge of first-aid and the possibilities that we have to tackle the situation.

During loading and discharging, the ship is berthed alongside an installation and will, therefore, have qualified personnel at its disposal. But they often take time to arrive, and it is therefore important for our own safety to have sufficient knowledge of the properties of the cargo, injury treatment and the appropiate first-aid.

In the case of injury by suffocation or poisoning it is necessary to begin first-aid immediately without losing any time. Let treatment begin as soon as the casualty has been brought to safety. Every second counts. Do not waste time transporting the patient to the sick-bay, but begin first-aid on the spot.

Instant treatment must begin when the casualty suffers:

unconsciousness
stopped breathing
heart failure.

A good rule to follow is the so-called ABC rule, which gives the order of first-aid:

A = Airway (free airways)
B = Breathing (ensure that the patient is breathing)
C = Circulation (ensure that the heart is beating)

Forget about making the patient comfortable. Do not put anything soft under the patient's head. Instead bend the patient's head backwards after having removed vomit, blood or dentures.It is this method that can save lives.

If the tongue has blocked the upper airways, it must be pulled free from the back of the mouth wall so that the passage to the lungs is open. The airways must be unblocked if life is to be saved. If anything is put under the head of the injured person, the airways will be blocked and the patient may die of lack of air.

When the patient is breathing again, the recovery position must be used. It is important to make it easier for the injured person to breath, and to avoid vomit etc. blocking the airways. The following diagrams show how an injured person is laid in the recovery position.

1. Push his nearest foot up against his buttocks so that his knee is at an angle.

2. Push his nearest hand under his buttocks.

3. Pull his free hand towards yourself so that he rolls onto his side. Guide with one hand on the patient's knee.

4. Bend his head backwards and lay his face low with his hand as support under the chin. Pull the other hand a little away from his back.

B - Artificial Respiration

If the unconscious person is still not breathing even after the airways are clear, artificial respiration must be begun. Most people know the principal of the mouth-to-mouth method, but again it is important to remember that the patient's head must lie well backwards when air is blown in at regular intervals, 12-16 times per minute.

Blow quickly 2-4 times and then check for any blood circulation. Can you feel any pulse in the carotid artery (on the side of the throat)? If there is, continue mouth-to-mouth at 12-16 inflations per minute.


C - External Cardiac Massage

If the unconscious person is not breathing even after 2-3 inflations with the mouth-to-mouth method, or if the pulse cannot be felt and signs of death are evident, it is a sign that the heart has stopped and we must start with external cardiac massage.

Lay the patient on a hard surface and strike him hard on the chestbone over the heart. This can be enough to get the heart working again. If this does not work, external cardiac massage must be started immediately, and remember that no time must be wasted. THIS CONCERNS LIFE OR DEATH.

External Cardiac Massage


Place the unconscious person on a hard surface. Kneel down beside him. Find the correct point, the lower part of the chest bone. Place your hand carpus two finger widths above the lower point of the chest bone. Lift your fingers from the patient's chest and put the other hand on top of the first hand Then begin the heart compression by pressing down 80 - 100 times per minute..

External Cardiac Massage + Mouth-to-Mouth

The best method of life-saving is to combine mouth-to-mouth and heart compression at the same time. Training is required to do this effectively.

During external cardiac massage blood is pumped around the arteries to bring oxygen to the higher centres of the brain, as described earlier in this letter. The air that is in the patient's lungs is oxygen starved, and without replacement of this air in the lungs no oxygen will be supplied to the blood.

We should practice the combination of giving mouth-to-mouth alternating with heart compression, with either 2 inflations and 15 heart compressions or 1 inflation and 5 heart compressions.

It is far better if two can work together in life-saving, as the treatment must continue and not stop too early. There are instances where unconscious persons have begun to breath after having been treated like this for several hours. As long as air is supplied to the lungs at the same time as heart compression is carried out, oxygen is distributed around in the body and life is maintained..

Look carefully at Fig. 19 which shows how mouth-to-mouth and heart compression are combined.

Generally we can say that we treat casualties and unconscious persons on board according to the following procedure:

1. Check breathing, and if he is breathing lay him in the recovery position.

2. If he is not breathing:

Check the airways, clear them (the tongue is blocking, do not put a pillow under the neck).
Turn onto the side, remove any slime and pull the tongue forward so breathing can restart unaided.

3. If he still does not start breathing, help must be given:

Mouth-to-mouth (inflating every 5 seconds)
Heart compression (press once every second)
Combination of heart compression and mouth-to-mouth (1 inflation of lungs followed by 5 heart compressions)

Treating General Injuries On Board

Fortunately it is seldom that accidents on board result in injuries that may lead to unconsciousness and eventual death. Most injuries that are a result of contact with cold cargoes in vapour or liquid form, are more likely to cause local damage to the skin and eyes.

We shall look closer at these injuries and at what can be done on board. The data sheet we saw in Letter 3 also contains information on what we can do to effectively reduce injuries caused by the cargo.

Inhalation Damage

We must first, of course, remove the injured person from the polluted area, and remember to use compressed air breathing apparatus during the operation so that we do not breath in the dangerous atmosphere.

The injured person should be laid down and kept warm. If the affected person has breathing difficulties, a sitting position may be more comfortable. In cases of breathing difficulties and the patient also having a blue-grey skin complection, it may be possible that the airways are not completely open. If this occurs, it must be investigated and the foreign object removed. (It may also be the tongue that is causing the blockage - bring it forward!).

If the airways are open, oxygen can be given, the equipment is found in the medicine chest. Such equipment must only be used by personnel that are familiar with its use.

Inhaling vapour from certain cargoes can also result in corrosive injuries in the mouth. The mouth, tongue and throat will be red with an appearance like raw meat. Such patients must always be transported to hospital and be treated by a qualified doctor because there will be a danger of collapse of the aesophagus or stomach walls. It is important to remember that such a patient must not vomit, and a stomach pump must never be used. In the meantime we must do our best to neutralize the corrosive liquid so that the area of damage is limited.

1. Rinse the mouth out, gurgle the throat if possible.

2. Give the patient half a litre of water to drink

3. Rinse the mouth and gurgle the throat again and ensure thorough rinsing between the teeth, under the tongue and the outside of the gums.

4. Check the first-aid instructions given on the relevant cargo data sheet. Contact a doctor for further treatment.

5. Keep the patient resting. Give pain killing medicine.

6. If the patient has been in contact with an acid, then give some neutralising drugs e.g. Balancid or Titralac. If the injury is due to a base (e.g. ammonia), give diluted lime juice or vinegar.

There is a great risk that anyone being exposed to gas may have accute cramp in the vocal chords, which can result in suffocation. When giving respiration to such a person it is important that we do not stop the treatment too early. The fact is that, just before death, such cramp will ease and the airways will open. Never stop respiration too early.

It is possible that someone giving mouth-to-mouth to a patient who has been exposed to toxic gas may himself be poisoned by the same gas. This has happened before, so if such a risk is possible respiration should be given by oxygen respirator.

All corrosive substances are local irritants, and on board a gas-ship ammonia (NH3) and chlorine (Cl2) are such gases that become corrosive when in contact with moisture.

The whole airway from the mouth opening and the nose down to the lungs is covered by moisture. The wall is thin and easy to damage. It is this area that is easily damaged by corrosive substances, with a risk of contracting oedema pulmonum. The membrane that covers the lungs on the inside can thicken due to the cicatrization that results from inhaling corrosive substances, which can again lead to swelling (oedema pulmonum) due to heart failure. This can appear as late as 12-24 hours after exposure. If oedema pulmonum occurs we can recognise this by the patient breathing quickly and heavily and the saliva being frothy mixed with traces of blood. In our treatment of corrosive substances in the air-ways we have to take the above into consideration and take steps to aviod and fight it.

1. Sit the patient well up in bed supported with pillows.

2. Give oxygen, 4-6 litres per minute.

3. Give steroid injection in the muscles (Solu-Corted 100-200mg)

4. Give one tablet Nuelin Retard.

5. Do not give any liquid, the patient must be dried out the first day.

6. Keep the patient warm and calm. Ensure that he gets sufficient fresh air.

If at this point we can notice tendencies towards oedema pulmonum

7. Give 50mg Pethidin in the musculature + 1 tablet Nuelin Retard.

8. Increase the oxygen supply to 10-20 litres per minute.

9. If the patient is still restless after one hour, give yet another injection of Pethidin.

10. Act calmly so that the patient also calms down. Encourage the patient to breath steadily. Check that the patient does not get cramp in the throat; if it occurs, give respiration. Remember not to stop life-saving too early.

Skin Damage

The skin reacts to corrosive substances in the same way as to hot and cold cargoes (in vapour or liquid form) and will, as mentioned earlier, give the same injuries as from the heat of a fire.

The extent of the injury follows the so-called 9% rule, where each arm and the head is calculated as 9% of the total body. Each leg, the front and back of the body is 18%. We reckon that those with injuries greater that 40% are difficult to save, even when the injured person is taken directly to qualified medics. We split burn injuries into grade of burns:

1st degree burns: Expanded blood vessels, red inflamed skin, pain.

2nd degree burns: Liquid separation, blisters, pain.

3rd degree burns: Dead skin, grey yellow colouring, no feeling before at a later stage.

Every time the skin is exposed to cold cargoes, it must be rinsed with water at once. Use the showers situated in the dangerous gas areas. Wash carefully with soap.

Remove clothing and ensure that the areas that were covered by them are also rinsed and eventually washed with soap and water. Do not stop showering to take off clothes, but stand under the shower whilst others help to remove clothing.

Most cases of gas leakages on board will pose no great risk of injury, but if a larger spillage occurs we must assess what can be done and how wide ranging the injuries are. Initially a wash with water under a shower is most important, until we have an overview of the injuries and can assess what must then be done.

In case of serious injuries we must follow the guide lines given for first-aid to casualties from fire:

1. Control the ABC functions.

2. Corrosive injuries: Wash/rinse with plentiful supplies of water, preferably at 200C for 15 minutes (with NH3 or Cl2 we must rinse for at least 30 minutes). Remove clothing and boots during washing.

Fire/Frost injuries: Rinse with plentiful supplies of water to avoid burning/freezing reaching further through the tissue. This reduces the pain. Rinse for 15 - 30 min. with water at abt 200C. Remove clothes and boots.

3. Keep the patient calm. Give painkillers as required.

4. Protect damaged skin with medical gauze. Remember that ointments, powder etc. are not to be used.

5. Keep the patient warm with wool blankets, dry clothing etc.

6. Give oxygen at 2-5 litres per minute.

7. If the patient cannot be moved to hospital, or the journey takes more than 2 hours, we must give a liquid feed to avoid the patient going into shock. (See under shock, later in this letter).

Eye Damage

As poisonous substances can lead to blindness if in contact with the eyes, a quick reaction in case of cargo leaks or spillage is important.

Injury to the eyes is very painful and results in an intense pinching together of the eye lids. The soft and loose build of the eye lids can result in great swelling, which again makes it difficult to open the eyes for treatment. It is absolutely necessary to have access to the eyes for rinsing. The quicker the rinsing is begun the more the injuries are reduced.

Continued pain after 15 minutes' rinsing is a sign of serious eye damage. The rinsing must be continued and skilled medical help must be sought. If the cornea turns white and matt, serious eye damage has to be assumed.

We must check to see what is written on the cargo data sheets, so that the special properties of the cargo are considered during treatment of the injured. Otherwise we must give the following first-aid:

1. Continual rinsing must take place mediately for at least 15 minutes. Use the eye wash equipment that is found in the gas dangerous area. Whilst we are helping the patient to rinse his eyes, other persons should get medicines from the medicine chest.

Local anaesthetics such as Tetracin eye drops can help the rinsing, as this will make it easier to open the eyes. If the pain eases momentarily because of the eye drops, we must not stop rinsing, but keep on. After finishing rinsing the eyes out on deck, we can rinse further with an eye bath consisting of 2 teaspoon-fuls of salt mixed in one litre of boiled water.

2. If the injured person cannot be taken to hospital or to a doctor, we must continue rinsing for at least a day, as long as the pain continues. We can drip Chloramfenicol eye drops every hour, and after rinsing for at least one day to stop the pain, a sterile dressing should be put over the eyes.

Frost Damage and Hypothermia

Our body reacts to cold by contracting the small blood veins under the exposed skin. The result is that less heat is given off, and normal temperature is maintained within the body.

The reduced blood circulation makes it easier for the body to be subject to frost attack. The skin gradually becomes white and insensitive. Eventually the skin will reach the state of freezing, and such unvoluntary cooling of the body is called hypothermia.

On board a gas ship we can have incidents where a crew member by accident can be exposed to rapid cool-down if soaked with cold cargo. Such a cooling will be rapid. In addition to the injuries from the cold cargo we will have a general cool down of the affected area, but this will not be hypothermia.

However, we shall look at the danger points of hypothermia, as this occurs far more frequently than we may realise.

The thermometer does not tell the true and whole story in the case of low temperatures. The effective temperature decreases dramatically with increasing wind speed. Minus 100C feels more like minus 200C when the wind increases from calm wind to 4,5 metres per second (light breeze).

The reason is that the wind takes away the heat from the naked skin and clothes, so that the cooling effect is much greater than the air temperature alone.

Quick cooling is less dangerous than slow cooling. Slow cooling can occur when, for example, one is surprised by freak weather in the mountains, or one falls overboard from the ship and lies in the cold water for a while.

Fig. 20

Such cooling gradually creeps over us, and we do not become aware of it as it is painless.

The cooling that we can experience on board a gas ship will be freezing that gives similar injuries to those of burns, and are treated as such.

Shock

When a person is in a condition of shock, this can be observed by a combination of signs and symptons, the so-called shock syndrome, connected with weakness or collapse of the blood circulation.

Signs that indicate a shock condition include amongst others, limpness, characteristic paleness, cold and moist skin (pale grey), weak and fast pulse, irregular and shallow breathing, thirst, nausea, reduced urine secretion and low blood pressure.

Causes can be heavy blood losses, loss of body fluid or salt e.g. due to serious fire injuries, frost damage, poisoning, reflective physical reaction or it can also mean cardiac arrest.

In serious accidents on board we can consider shock victims. During treatment of the injured it is best to be aware of any shock signs and symtoms. In all shock cases (except cardiac arrest) liquid supply is very important, as well as life-saving. As all body liquids contain salt, it is important that any liquid given to shock victims contains salt.

In cases of shock, the following first-aid should be given:

1. Encourage the patient to breath deeply, and give him a feeling of self-confidence and calmness.

2. Lay the patient flat and elevate his legs (place a large pillow under his legs).

3. Give oxygen, 5-10 litres per minute.

4. Give painkillers if the patient is in pain.

5. Keep the patient warm and calm.

6. Give liquid as a drink or enema.

Liquid as drink can include bovril, mineral water, tea with sugar. Nausea often leads to vomiting. Still try to give 1 dessert spoonful of liquid every 5 minutes, but in these situations enema must be given to maintain the liquid balance.

Enema liquid consists of 2 teaspoonfuls of salt dissolved in 1 litre of luke warm water. The first litre should be administered within the first half hour, thereafter according to liquid/salt loss.

However, a total of at least 2 litres of water has to be given per day, and if the patient has a fever, the liquid should be increased by abt 10% per day.

A shock condition can occur suddenly and get worse quickly if preventive measures are not started soon. Anyone who has been seriously injured or burnt/frozen should be kept under observation for shock for several hours.

The term shock is often used for conditions that do not have any simularities with the shock syndrome, but may be serious and require immediate attention.

The term physical shock is used to describe the condition of confusion and despair which can be seen in persons that have been involved in an accident or a large personal tragedy. We must also be on guard for such conditions on board when there has been an accident that has resulted in serious injuries.

Health Risks etc From Various Gases/Substances

The cargoes that we carry on board can, if a leak occurs, be a health hazard to the crew. The cargo will either leak out as vapour (e.g. a leak in the compressor room) or as liquid (e.g. sudden leak in cargo hose). We are, therefore, exposed to a possible risk of injury from cold liquid (frost damage) or poisoning from the vapour (suffocation).

Smaller leakages within the cargo handling plant can also lead to personnel injuries. When certain gases have properties that are dangerous to the health, such leakages must be avoided. A typical example is the risk of liver damage that can be caused by steady and regular exposure to vapour from VCM cargo.

By mixing different substances e.g. with air, new chemical substances can be formed. Such cargoes will have inhibitors added to reduce this risk. Odourless cargoes may be mixed with an additive to give them a distinctive smell, hence making it easier to discover leakages.

Certain gases that are exposed to high temperatures can also start a reaction, and new chemicals that are poisonous and corrosive may form. Cargoes exposed to high temperature will increase the vapour pressure and thereby increase the risk of leakages within the cargo handling plant.

When cargo tanks are insulated with polyurethane, a fire in this insulation would produce poisonous gases, that again represents a health risk for the crew.

We shall now look at some of the gases we transport, and other substances that may represent a health hazard.

BUTADIENE is one of the gases where an inhibitor has to be added to prevent reaction with oxygen. To prevent oxygen from entering the cargo tank, a nitrogen padding is necessary to maintain an overpressure throughout the transportation. (This is not absolutely necessary if the cargo is transported at ambient temperature in a warm climate).

At relatively low concentrations butadiene will give discomfort, and at 2000 - 4000 ppm we will get a strong irritation in the eyes and difficulty in focusing. Norway has suggested that the TLV for this product should be as low as 1 ppm.

VINYL CHLORIDE MONOMER (VCM) belongs to the unsaturated halogen hydrocarbons. Great care has to be taken to avoid leakages from this cargo. The TLV is very low as the substance is cancerous even in small quantities. In the USA no more than 25 ppm VCM concentration is allowed anywhere on board during cargo operation. At international level, a proposal has been put forward to fix a new TLV for VCM at zero.

VCM is absorbed by the lungs and gives dizziness at concentrations between 2000 and 5000ppm. At concentrations up to 16000 ppm, hearing and sight will be affected and over 120000ppm VCM is deadly.

If constantly inhaled, even in small quantities, we can get the typical VCM syndrome of "white fingers", which is normally a reaction in occupations were the hands are subject to strong regular vibrations.

ACETALDEHYDE vapour on inhaling gives water in the lungs. We saw in Letter 4 that the ship must be equipped with extra air breathing apparatus if we are to carry this cargo. The only method of treatment for any person having swallowed acetadehyde is to have the lungs emptied for water at a hospital, but on board the ship we can only sit the person up so that the water collected in the lungs does not result in death. Otherwise there is nothing we can do apart from giving respiration to reduce discomfort and pain.

If anyone gets a "sniff" of acetaldehyde they will get red eyes, the same "day-after" effect we know from secretation of alcohol from the body. The same symptoms are also known from the use of "Antabus".

AMMONIA has a strong uncomfortable and suffocating smell that takes the breath away from us. If we get a strong sniff of NH3 we must rinse our mouth with water and rinse through the nose to reduce the effect on the mucous membrane by letting the ammonia be absorbed by water.

CHLORINE in vapour form has a yellow-green colour and a heavy smell. The gas reacts with water and forms an acid.

In cases of acute attack by chlorine we get an irritation on the mucous membrane, the eyes, nose and throat. This increases and becomes painful and a burning sensation is felt, particularly in the chest. We get reflex coughing and pain behind the chest bone. In serious attacks, death will result from suffocation as the chlorine gives cramp in the throat muscles and swelling.

SULPHUR DIOXIDE is a colourless gas with a strong smell and a sourish taste. It reacts easily with water to form an acid as well as with body liquids.

The danger is accute at high concentrations and will give breathing difficulties. Cramp in the throat muscles is also possible, leading to suffocation.

With such injuries the patient must be kept completely calm, especially the physical first-aid is important to establish a trusty relationship. Remember what we have been through earlier regarding not stopping artificial respiration too early, because the paralysing feeling in the throat can be reduced just before death.

In cases of damage to the eyes we must rinse throroughly and use a 0-tip to get a good clean and effective rinse.

PRUSSIC ACID. On board a gas ship fire in polyurethane insulation produces calcium cyanide which causes heavy smoke and a stinking smell.

This smoke (Prussic acid) is absorbed directly through the skin to the blood, which restricts the oxygen supply via the blood. The skin becomes light-red and we can notice the bitter almond smell from the patient's breath. Such poisoning requires very quick actions as the patient will get headaches, dizziness, breathing difficulties, suffocation and cramp in a very short time, and death will occur after only a few minutes.

First-aid treatment should start first and foremost by getting the injured person away from the dangerous area, control the ABC functions and begin respiration at the same time as washing the patient. Also break one Amylnitrite capsule in a cloth and hold it up towards the mouth and nose at a distance of abt 3 cm for about 20 seconds.

CARBON MONOXIDE (GO) is an odourless gas that is produced when organic material are not completely burnt (incomplete combustion).The gas is heavy and tends to collect down on the floor (the lowest point in the area).

Anyone who has inhaled this gas will get a blue-red colour, an increasingly sever headache, breathing difficulties and become tired and dizzy. The carbon monoxide combines with the hemoglobin in the blood and renders the latter incapable of transporting the oxygen, leading to an extreme oxygen shortage that must be compensated by giving respiration. This takes several hours to correct, hence respiration will be needed throughout.

The right treatment is to get the person out in fresh air and avoid letting him exert himself as there is a significant oxygen shortage in the blood. Give oxygen in plentiful amounts, 8 - 10 litres per minute, and continue this treatment for a few hours. Oxygen scarcity is continuously a great risk to the functioning of the heart. As a normal rule it can be reckoned that brain damage might occur if the oxygen is stopped for more than 4 minutes.

Conclusion - Emergency First-Aid Procedure after Cargo Spillage

Liquid in the eye: Rinse the eye carefully with water. Force the eye open if necessary. Continue the rinsing for at least 15 minutes.

Liquid on the skin: Shower the affected area, remove clothing. With poisonous cargoes continue showering for at least 15 minutes. With frost injuries rinse with cold water.

Inhaled vapour: Take the patient into fresh air, control the ABC functions and give artificial respiration/ oxygen. With ammonia and chlorine give respiration with oxygen.

Everybody on board should be able to give first-aid. Train in doing exercises and make the conditions as realistic as possible. Practice the use of the ship's oxygen set and ensure that all on board are able to execute mouth-to-mouth and external cardiac massage.
